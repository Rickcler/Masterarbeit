\chapter*{Abstract}
\addcontentsline{toc}{chapter}{Abstract}

Categorical time series arise in numerous applied settings, yet the analysis of such
data in the presence of missing observations has received comparatively little
methodological attention. Building on the amplitude modulation framework introduced
by \citet{weiss2021categorical}, this thesis develops asymptotic theory for distributional
measures of ordinal and nominal categorical time series under missingness. For both
measures of marginal dispersion and measures of serial dependence, 
missingness-corrected estimators are introduced, and their asymptotic normality is established 
under certain dependency conditions. The delta method is used to derive closed-form expressions for the
asymptotic variance and the leading-order finite-sample bias of these estimators, and
a hypothesis test for serial independence based on the corrected $\kappa$-estimator is
constructed. The accompanying Monte Carlo simulation study, based on the BinAR(1) process,
goes beyond the illustrations in \citet{weiss2021categorical} by systematically
investigating finite-sample accuracy and rates of convergence across a broad range
of sample sizes, and by extending the analysis to more complex missingness structures:
in addition to the i.i.d.\ MCAR baseline, we examine serially dependent observation
processes that remain independent of the underlying categorical process, as well as
two state-dependent MNAR mechanisms in which the observation probability depends on
the current value of the process, thereby assessing the robustness of the proposed
methods under violations of the MCAR assumption. The results show that the corrected 
estimators and the proposed test perform well 
under MCAR even when the observation process exhibits serial dependence. Under MNAR, 
however, the estimators inherit a persistent bias that does not vanish with growing 
sample size, and the test can exhibit severe size distortion or substantial loss of 
power -- underscoring the practical value of the amplitude modulation framework and 
the importance of carefully assessing the MCAR assumption before applying the 
proposed methods.

\chapter*{Introduction}
\addcontentsline{toc}{chapter}{Introduction}

Categorical time series arise naturally in many empirical disciplines. 
In the social sciences, survey respondents repeatedly assess their 
satisfaction on an ordered scale; in medicine, patient outcomes are often recorded on categorical scales,
as in the study of sleep states by \citet{Stoffer2000}; in 
meteorology, daily precipitation rates may be analyzed as ordered 
categories with temporal dependence like in \citet{Chang1984}. What these settings have 
in common is that the observed variable at each time point takes 
values in a finite set of categories, and that successive observations 
are not independent. The statistical analysis of such data requires 
tools that are tailored to the categorical nature of the observations 
and that account for the temporal dependence structure of the process. \\
Categorical time series are a special case of the broader class of 
discrete-valued time series surveyed by \citet{Weiss2018}. However, while 
certain discrete valued processes, like count-valued processes, 
may  admit a natural numerical interpretation that 
allows arithmetic operations on the observed values, categorical 
variables generally don't carry any such meaning. Consider school grades 
as a typical example. Although the categories are often labelled numerically, 
these labels merely reflect the underlying ordering and do not support meaningful 
arithmetic operations. Consequently, standard tools such as means, variances, or 
autocorrelations are generally inappropriate and must be replaced by measures tailored to categorical data.\\
Missing observations are a common challenge across empirical 
disciplines. In cross-sectional surveys, respondents may refuse to 
answer certain questions or give incomplete responses; in automated 
monitoring systems, sensor failures or transmission errors render 
individual time points unobservable. In longitudinal surveys, respondents 
may forget or be unavailable during certain waves of data collection. 
Ignoring such gaps and computing naive estimators from the observed 
data alone can introduce substantial bias and lead to wrong conclusions. 
The classical taxonomy of missingness mechanisms, introduced by 
\citet{Rubin1976} and further developed by \citet{little_rubin_2002}, 
provides the conceptual foundation for a principled treatment of 
incomplete data. While many of these ideas carry over conceptually to the time series 
setting, the sequential structure of categorical processes and the 
absence of meaningful arithmetic operations call for a dedicated 
treatment that respects the specific nature of the data. 
However, although the statistical analysis of complete categorical time series
is well established, considerably less attention has been devoted to situations 
in which observations are missing.\\
The starting point for the present thesis is the work of 
\citet{weiss2021categorical}, who addresses the analysis of 
categorical time series in the presence of missing observations --- 
a setting for which only limited methodological results had previously been available. 
Both ordinal and nominal processes are considered, 
and solutions are developed for the analysis of marginal and serial 
properties under missingness. This is achieved by adapting the concept 
of amplitude modulation to the categorical setting: a binary process 
$(O_t)$ runs concurrently with the categorical process $(X_t)$, with 
$O_t = 1$ indicating that $X_t$ is observed and $O_t = 0$ indicating 
that it is missing. Furthermore, closed-form 
expressions for the asymptotic distributions of the derived statistics are 
obtained. Throughout most of the theoretical development,
missingness is assumed to occur completely at random,
implying independence between the observation process and the
underlying categorical process.\\
The present thesis builds on this foundation in several ways. First, 
we provide a more explicit and self-contained derivation of the 
asymptotic results, spelling out the underlying mixing assumptions and 
the CLT arguments in full detail. Second, we conduct an extensive 
Monte Carlo simulation study that goes beyond the illustrations in 
\citet{weiss2021categorical} by systematically investigating the 
finite-sample behavior and the rate of convergence of the proposed 
estimators. Third, we examine the effect of serially dependent 
missingness, where $(O_t)$ is no longer i.i.d.\ but follows a 
stationary binary Markov chain, and study how temporal dependence in 
the observation process affects the asymptotic variance of the 
estimators. Finally, we investigate the robustness of the proposed 
methods under violations of the assumption of independence of $O_t$ and $X_t$, considering 
state-dependent missingness mechanisms where the probability of 
observation depends on the current value of the underlying process 
$(X_t)$.\\
The thesis is structured as follows. \Cref{chap:distributional} 
presents the theoretical framework for categorical time series, by introducing 
basic concepts that relate to time-series, like  stationarity, as well as 
concepts that are specific to categorical data, like measures of location and 
dispersion, and Cohen's $\kappa$ as a measure of serial dependence. 
\Cref{chap:sample} develops estimators for the relevant measures based on counting relative frequencies, 
and develops the asymptotic theory for these sample-based quantities in the fully observed case. 
\Cref{chap:missingness} introduces the amplitude modulation framework, 
derives the corrected estimators and their asymptotic properties, and 
develops the hypothesis test for serial independence. 
\Cref{chap:simulation} presents the simulation study. 
\Cref{chap:nominal} presents the corresponding theory for nominal processes. 
Technical derivations are collected in the Appendix.


\chapter{Distributional Properties of Categorical Processes}\label{chap:distributional}

\section{General Notation}

Let  $(X_t)$ be a \textit{categorical}  time series process, where each $X_t$ takes values in

\[
S := \{s_0, \dots, s_m\}, \qquad m \in \mathbb{N}.
\]
$(X_t)$ may be \textit{nominal}, in which case the order of magnitude of the elements in $S$ is arbitrary and solely used for notational consistency, or $ordinal$, in which case the order of the elements in $S$ reflects the inherent order of the elements.
\section{Stationarity}
Furthermore, $(X_t)$ is assumed to be stationary. A distinction can be made between two forms of stationarity:
\begin{definition}[Weak Stationarity]
    A process $(X_t)$ is said to be \emph{weakly stationary} if:
        \[
            \begin{aligned}
            \mathrm{E}(X_t) &= \mu_X \quad &&\forall t, \\
            \mathrm{E}(X_t^2) &< \infty \quad &&\forall t, \\
            \mathrm{Cov}(X_t, X_{t+h}) &= \gamma(h) \quad &&\forall t,h.
            \end{aligned}
        \]
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Strong/ Strict Stationarity]\label{def:Stationarity}
    Strong/ Strict stationarity requires that all finite-dimensional distributions are invariant under time shifts, i.e.
        \begin{equation*}
            F_{X_t,\ldots,X_{t+j}} = F_{X_{t+h},\ldots,X_{t+h+j}} \quad \forall\,t,j,h.
        \end{equation*}
\end{definition}
\section{Marginal Distributions}

Regardless of whether we consider a simple categorical process with few categories or attempt to describe a complex, temporally evolving process using moments and measures of serial dependence, the analysis of categorical processes is always built on the examination of the marginal probabilities. However, because ordinal and nominal data encode different types of information, their marginals are typically represented in different ways:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Ordinal data:}
    Most analytical tools for ordinal processes take into account the natural ordering of the elements in $S$. For this reason, most methods for ordinal data rely on the marginal cumulative distribution function (CDF), which accumulates probabilities in accordance with the natural ordering of the categories:
    \[
        \boldsymbol{f} = (f_0, \dots, f_{m-1})^\top,
        \qquad
        f_i := P(X_t \le s_i).
    \]

    When working with $\boldsymbol{f}$ it is sufficient to consider the cumulative probabilities of the categories $0, \ldots , m-1$ since the cumulative probability of the last category $m$ is always equal to 1. \\

    Although one could equivalently estimate the PMF and derive the CDF from it, representing ordinal data through the CDF is often more convenient as it this form that is used to compute metrics for location and dispersion.

    \item \textbf{Nominal data:}
    Since the categories of nominal variables do not possess a meaningful order, statistical procedures cannot make use of cumulative structure.
    Therefore, the marginal distribution is best represented by the probability mass function (PMF):
    \[
        \boldsymbol{p} = (p_0, \dots, p_{m})^\top,
        \qquad
        p_i := P(X_t = s_i).
    \]
\end{itemize}


\section{Bivariate Probabilities at Lag $h$}

\begin{definition}[Bivariate lag-$h$ probabilities]
\label{def:biv_probs}
Let $(X_t)$ be a stationary categorical time series. The bivariate lag-$h$ 
probabilities are defined as follows.
\begin{enumerate}
    \item[\normalfont(i)] \textbf{Ordinal series:}
    \[
    f_{ij}(h) := P(X_t \le s_i,\, X_{t+h} \le s_j), \qquad i,j = 0,\ldots, m-1.
    \]
    \item[\normalfont(ii)] \textbf{Nominal series:}
    \[
    p_{ij}(h) := P(X_t = s_i,\, X_{t+h} = s_j), \qquad i,j = 0,\ldots, m.
    \]
\end{enumerate}
Under serial independence, the bivariate probabilities factorize as 
$f_{ij}(h) = f_i f_j$ and $p_{ij}(h) = p_ip_j$ respectively.
\end{definition}
\section{Location}

For a nominal process, any measure that relies on ordering is inappropriate.
Instead, a location measure must depend only on the category probabilities.
The mode, defined as the most probable category,
\[
\operatorname{Mod}(X_t) = \arg\max_{s_i \in S} P(X_t = s_i),
\]
is therefore the natural choice. It provides a meaningful summary of the process by identifying the most frequent category.\\
For an ordinal process, the natural ordering of the categories allows for additional measures of central tendency.
While the mode can still be informative as the most frequent category, the median respects the ordering of categories and is often more useful for describing the central location:
\[
\operatorname{Med}(X_t)
=
\min \bigl\{\, s_i \in S : f_i \ge \tfrac{1}{2} \,\bigr\}.
\]
Thus, for ordinal data, both the mode and the median carry information, but they capture slightly different aspects of location: the mode identifies the most typical category, whereas the median indicates the midpoint in the ordered scale.
\section{Measures of Dispersion}

Since the concept of dispersion differs between ordinal and nominal processes, two separate measures are employed: the \emph{Index of Ordinal Variation} (IOV) for ordinal data and the \emph{Index of Qualitative Variation} (IQV) for nominal data, defined as
\begin{equation*}\label{eq:iov_iqv}
    \mathrm{IOV}(\boldsymbol{f}) = \frac{4}{m}\sum_{i=0}^{m-1}f_i(1-f_i), \qquad
    \mathrm{IQV}(\boldsymbol{p}) = \frac{m+1}{m}\!\left(1-\sum_{i=0}^{m}p_i^2\right).
\end{equation*}
Both indices are scaled to $[0,1]$, with $0$ indicating a degenerate (one-point) distribution and $1$ indicating maximal dispersion. However, what constitutes maximal dispersion differs: for ordinal data, dispersion is maximal when all probability mass is concentrated in the two outermost categories, whereas for nominal data it corresponds to the uniform distribution over all $m+1$ categories.
\begin{proposition}[Dispersion Bounds]\label{prop:dispersion_bounds}
Both $\mathrm{IOV}$ and $\mathrm{IQV}$ satisfy:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item[\normalfont(i)] $\mathrm{IOV}=0$ (resp.\ $\mathrm{IQV}=0$) if and only if
    the distribution is degenerate.
    \item[\normalfont(ii)] $\mathrm{IOV}=1$ iff
    $\boldsymbol{f}=(\tfrac{1}{2},\ldots,\tfrac{1}{2})^\top$;
    $\mathrm{IQV}=1$ iff $p_i=\tfrac{1}{m+1}$ for all $i$.
\end{enumerate}
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
\textit{Part (i), ordinal case.} % dein bestehender Beweis fÃ¼r IOV=0
A one-point distribution assigns all probability mass to a single category
$s_j \in S$.
For ordinal data, this corresponds to a CDF vector $\mathbf{f}$ of the form
\[
\mathbf{f} \in
\left\{
\begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 1 \\ \vdots \\ 1 \end{pmatrix},
\begin{pmatrix} 0 \\ 1 \\ \vdots \\ 1 \end{pmatrix},
\ldots,
\begin{pmatrix} 0 \\ 0 \\ \vdots \\ 1 \end{pmatrix}
\right\}
:= \{\boldsymbol{c}_0,\ldots,\boldsymbol{c}_m\} \subset [0,1]^{m}.
\]
In each of these cases, every component $f_i$ satisfies $f_i \in \{0,1\}$, and thus
\[
f_i(1 - f_i) = 0 \qquad \forall i.
\]
Therefore,
\[
\mathrm{IOV}
= \frac{4}{m}\sum_{i=0}^{m-1} f_i (1 - f_i)
= 0.
\]
Conversely, if $\mathrm{IOV}=0$, then
\[
\sum_{i=0}^{m-1} f_i(1 - f_i) = 0,
\]
and since each term is nonnegative,
\[
f_i(1 - f_i) \overset{!}{=} 0 \quad \forall i.
\]
This is only possible if $f_i \in \{0,1\}$ for all $i$, which implies
$\mathbf{f} \in \{\boldsymbol{e}_0,\ldots,\boldsymbol{e}_m\}$.
Hence, $\mathrm{IOV}=0$ holds \emph{exactly} for a degenerate one-point distribution.\\\\
\textit{Part (i), nominal case.} % dein bestehender Beweis fÃ¼r IQV=0
A degenerate (one--point) distribution assigns all probability mass to a single
category $s_j \in S$, which corresponds to a PMF vector of the form
\[
\mathbf{p} \in
\left\{
\begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \\ \vdots \\ 0 \end{pmatrix},
\begin{pmatrix} 0 \\ 1 \\ \vdots \\ 0 \end{pmatrix},
\ldots,
\begin{pmatrix} 0 \\ 0 \\ \vdots \\ 1 \end{pmatrix}
\right\}
:= \{\boldsymbol{e}_0,\ldots,\boldsymbol{e}_m\} \subset [0,1]^{m+1}.
\]
In each of these cases, exactly one component equals $1$ and all others equal $0$, so
\[
\sum_{i=0}^m p_i^2 = 1.
\]
Thus,
\[
\mathrm{IQV}
= \frac{m+1}{m}\bigl(1 - \sum_{i=0}^m p_i^2\bigr)
= \frac{m+1}{m}(1 - 1) = 0.
\]
Conversely, if $\mathrm{IQV} = 0$, then
\[
\frac{m+1}{m}\Bigl(1 - \sum_{i=0}^m p_i^2\Bigr) = 0
\quad\Longrightarrow\quad
\sum_{i=0}^m p_i^2 = 1.
\]
As all probabilities satisfy $0 \leq p_i \leq 1$, it follows that \[
p_i^2 \leq p_i \quad \forall p_i, \text{ and } p_i^2 < p_i \quad \forall p_i \notin \{0,1\}.
\]
Since $\sum_{i=1}^{m}p_i = 1$, it follows that the only way $\sum_i p_i^2 = 1$ can hold is if exactly one $p_i$ equals 1 and all others are 0. In other words, the distribution is degenerate and $\boldsymbol{p} \in \{\boldsymbol{e}_0,\ldots, \boldsymbol{e}_{m}\}$.\\
\textit{Part (ii), ordinal case.} % dein bestehender Beweis fÃ¼r IOV=1
Dispersion of an ordinal variable is maximal when half of the
probability mass lies in the lowest category and half in the highest
category; with all intermediate categories having probability zero.
This results in a CDF vector,

\[
\boldsymbol{f} = \left(\tfrac{1}{2}, \tfrac{1}{2}, \ldots, \tfrac{1}{2}\right)^\top.
\]
Then
\[
f_i(1 - f_i) = \frac{1}{2}\left(1 - \frac{1}{2}\right) = \frac{1}{4} \qquad \forall i,
\]
and thus
\[
\mathrm{IOV}
= \frac{4}{m} \cdot \sum_{i=0}^{m-1} \frac{1}{4}
= \frac{4}{m} \cdot \frac{m}{4}
= 1.
\]
To show that $\mathrm{IOV} = 1 \Rightarrow \boldsymbol{f} = (\tfrac{1}{2}, \ldots, \tfrac{1}{2})^T$, recall that the IOV only depends on $\boldsymbol{f}$ through the sum
\[
\sum_{i=0}^{m-1} f_i (1 - f_i).
\]
Taking the derivative of $g(f_i) := f_i(1-f_i)$  and setting it to zero yields:
\begin{align*}
\frac{\partial}{\partial f_i} g(f_i) &= 1 - 2 f_i, \\
1 - 2 f_i &\overset{!}{=} 0, \\
f_i &= \frac{1}{2}.
\end{align*}
Since $g(f_i=0) = g(f_i = 1)=0$, the function is concave and this critical point is indeed the global maximum.
Since it has been already shown that $\mathrm{IOV}(\boldsymbol{f} = (\frac{1}{2}, \ldots, \frac{1}{2})^T) = 1$, it directly follows that
\[
\mathrm{IOV}(\boldsymbol{f}) = 1 \Leftrightarrow \boldsymbol{f} = (\frac{1}{2}, \ldots, \frac{1}{2})^\top
\]
\textit{Part (ii), nominal case.} % dein bestehender Beweis fÃ¼r IQV=1
For nominal data, maximal dispersion is attained when probability mass is distributed uniformly across all $m+1$ categories, i.e.
\[
p_i = \frac{1}{m+1}, \qquad i = 0,\ldots,m.
\]
In this case,
\[
\sum_{i=0}^m p_i^2 = (m+1)\frac{1}{(m+1)^2} = \frac{1}{m+1},
\]
and hence
\[
\mathrm{IQV}
= \frac{m+1}{m}\left(1 - \frac{1}{m+1}\right)
= 1.
\]
Conversely, suppose that $\mathrm{IQV} = 1$. Then
\[
\frac{m+1}{m}\left(1 - \sum_{i=0}^m p_i^2\right) = 1
\quad\Longrightarrow\quad
\sum_{i=0}^m p_i^2 = \frac{1}{m+1}.
\]
Thus, maximizing $\mathrm{IQV}$ is equivalent to minimizing
\[
f(\boldsymbol p) := \sum_{i=0}^m p_i^2
\quad \text{subject to} \quad
\sum_{i=0}^m p_i = 1.
\]
Introducing the Lagrangian (with Lagrange multiplier $\lambda$)
\[
\mathcal L(\boldsymbol p, \lambda)
:= \sum_{i=0}^m p_i^2 - \lambda\left(\sum_{i=0}^m p_i - 1\right),
\]
the first-order conditions are
\begin{align*}
    \frac{\partial \mathcal L}{\partial p_j} = 2p_j - \lambda &= 0\\
    \Rightarrow \quad 2p_j &= \lambda, \qquad j=0,\dots,m,
\end{align*}
together with the constraint $\sum_{i=0}^m p_i = 1$.
Since the same multiplier $\lambda$ applies to all components, it follows that
\[
p_0 = p_1 = \dots = p_m.
\]
Imposing the constraint yields
\[
(m+1)p_j = 1 \quad \Longrightarrow \quad p_j = \frac{1}{m+1} \quad \forall j \in 0,\dots, m .
\]
Hence, $f(\boldsymbol p)$ is uniquely minimized at
\[
\boldsymbol p = \left(\tfrac{1}{m+1}, \dots, \tfrac{1}{m+1}\right)^\top,
\]
and therefore $\mathrm{IQV}$ attains its maximal value at the uniform distribution.

\end{proof}


\section{Measures of serial dependence}

The marginal distribution of a categorical time series captures the long-run 
frequencies of the individual categories, but contains no information about the 
temporal structure of the process. To describe how the process behaves over time, 
measures of serial dependence are required.\\
For real-valued processes, the autocorrelation function (ACF) is the standard 
tool for quantifying temporal dependence \citep[see Section 1.4]{brockwell_davis_2016}. However, the ACF is not directly applicable 
to categorical data, since it relies on the existence of meaningful arithmetic 
operations on the observed values. For nominal data, such operations are undefined, 
and for ordinal data they are questionable, as the categories carry no numerical meaning beyond their ordering.\\
Instead, serial dependence measures for categorical time series are typically constructed 
from the bivariate lag-$h$ probabilities introduced in Definition \ref{def:biv_probs}. 
Under serial independence, these would factorize as $f_{ij}(h) = f_if_j$ and 
$p_{ij}(h) = p_ip_j$ respectively. Measures for serial dependence are naturally 
constructed by quantifying the deviation of the bivariate probabilities from this 
independence benchmark.\\
Natural and widely used measures of serial dependence for categorical data are the ordinal 
and nominal versions of Cohen's $\kappa$ \citep[p. 130]{Weiss2018}, which compare the joint 
probabilities to those expected under independence. For lag $h$, they 
are defined as
\begin{definition}[Cohen's $\kappa$]
\label{def:cohens-kappa}

\[
    \kappa_{\mathrm{ord}}(h) 
    = \frac{\sum_{j=0}^{m-1}\bigl(f_{jj}(h) - f_j^2\bigr)}
           {\sum_{i=0}^{m-1} f_i(1-f_i)}
    \quad \text{and} \quad
    \kappa_{\mathrm{nom}}(h) 
    = \frac{\sum_{j=0}^{m}\bigl(p_{jj}(h) - p_j^2\bigr)}
           {1-\sum_{i=0}^{m} p_i^2}.
\]
\end{definition}
\noindent The numerator of each measure sums the diagonal deviations $f_{jj}(h) - f_j^2$
(resp.\ $p_{jj}(h) - p_j^2$), which capture the excess probability of observing
the same category at times $t$ and $t+h$ relative to what would be expected under
independence. The denominator normalizes the numerator by a measure of marginal dispersion:
for the ordinal version it equals $\frac{m}{4}\cdot\mathrm{IOV}$, and for the
nominal version it is equal to $\frac{m}{m+1}\cdot \mathrm{IQV}$. This ensures that the measures are invariant to
the overall level of dispersion in the marginal distribution. For both measures, positive values indicate that the process tends to remain in the same
category across lag $h$, while negative values indicate a tendency to switch
categories.\\
Under perfect positive dependence, i.e.\ $P(X_{t+h} = s_i \mid X_t = s_i) = 1$
for all $s_i \in S$, we have $f_{jj}(h) = f_j$ and $p_{jj}(h) = p_j$, and both
measures attain their upper bound:
\begin{align*}
    \kappa_{\mathrm{ord}}(h) 
    &= \frac{\sum_{j=0}^{m-1}(f_j - f_j^2)}{\sum_{i=0}^{m-1}(f_i - f_i^2)} = 1, \\[1ex]
    \kappa_{\mathrm{nom}}(h) 
    &= \frac{\sum_{j=0}^{m}(p_j - p_j^2)}{1 - \sum_{i=0}^{m}p_i^2}
    = \frac{1 - \sum_{j=0}^{m}p_j^2}{1 - \sum_{i=0}^{m}p_i^2} = 1,
\end{align*}
where we used that $\sum_{j=0}^{m} p_j = 1$, so that $\sum_{j=0}^{m}(p_j - p_j^2) = 1 - \sum_{j=0}^{m} p_j^2$. 
Under serial independence, $f_{jj}(h) = f_j^2$ and $p_{jj}(h) = p_jp_j$, so that
the numerator vanishes and $\kappa(h) = 0$.\\
For the nominal version, the lower bound is attained when $p_{jj}(h) = 0$ for all
$j$, i.e.\ when the process never remains in the same category across lag $h$:
\[
\kappa_{\mathrm{nom}}(h) 
= \frac{-\sum_{j=0}^{m}p_j^2}{1 - \sum_{i=0}^{m}p_i^2},
\]
which depends on the marginal distribution and equals $-1$ only in the special case
of a uniform marginal. For the ordinal version, the lower bound takes an analogous
form but involves the cumulative probabilities $f_j$.
\chapter{Sample Measures}\label{chap:sample}
\section{Estimation of Marginal Distributions}
To estimate the marginal (cumulative) probabilities, we express the
relative frequencies through a binarization of the original process
$(X_t)$. For ordinal data, define the indicator vector
\[
\boldsymbol{Z}_t := (Z_{t,0}, \ldots, Z_{t,m-1})^\top,
\qquad
Z_{t,i} := \mathbb{I}\{X_t \le s_i\},
\]
and for nominal data analogously
\[
\boldsymbol{Y}_t := (Y_{t,0}, \ldots, Y_{t,m})^\top,
\qquad
Y_{t,i} := \mathbb{I}\{X_t = s_i\}.
\]
Here, $\mathbb{I}\{A\}$ denotes the indicator function, which equals 1 if the event $A$ is true and 0 otherwise.
The marginal CDF and PMF vectors are estimated by the corresponding sample means:
\begin{definition}[Empirical CDF and PMF estimators]
\label{def:empirical_estimators}
\[
\hat{\boldsymbol{f}} := \frac{1}{n} \sum_{t=1}^n \boldsymbol{Z}_t,
\qquad
\hat{\boldsymbol{p}} := \frac{1}{n} \sum_{t=1}^n \boldsymbol{Y}_t.
\]
\end{definition}

\section{Asymptotics of the marginal estimator}
To derive the asymptotic distribution of $\hat{\boldsymbol{f}}$ and
$\hat{\boldsymbol{p}}$, a central limit theorem is
required. Although the derivations for the ordinal and nominal cases follow the same general strategy, 
they differ in several technical details. Therefore, we will mainly focus on the ordinal case and its 
asymptotic results in the following sections. The corresponding results for the nominal case are analogous 
and are presented in Chapter~\ref{chap:nominal}. \\ 
Since independence is not assumed, we impose weak dependence
conditions on the original process $(X_t)$.
Specifically, $(X_t)_{t \in \mathbb{Z}}$ is assumed to be strictly
stationary (recall Definition \ref{def:Stationarity}) and $\alpha$-mixing with exponentially decaying coefficients.\\
\begin{definition}[$\alpha$-Mixing with Exponential Decay]\label{def:mixing}
Following \citet[Section 1.2.]{bradley_2005}, let
\[
\mathcal{F}_J^L := \sigma(X_t : J \le t \le L)
\]
denote the $\sigma$-field generated by $\{X_t : J \le t \le L\}$.
The $\alpha$-mixing coefficients of $(X_t)$, given that it's strictly stationary, are defined as
\[
\alpha_{X_t}(n)
:= \sup_{A \in \mathcal{F}_{-\infty}^0,\; B \in \mathcal{F}_{n}^{\infty}}
\bigl| P(A \cap B) - P(A)P(B) \bigr|.
\]
The process is called $\alpha$-mixing if $\alpha(n) \to 0$ as $n \to \infty$.\\
We additionally assume exponential decay of the mixing coefficients,
i.e., there exist constants $C > 0$ and $\rho \in (0,1)$ such that
\begin{equation*}\label{eq:exp_mixing}
\alpha(n) \le C \rho^n \qquad \text{for all } n \in \mathbb{N}.
\end{equation*}
\end{definition}
\noindent
We now want to show that $\boldsymbol{Z}_t$ also satisfies these dependence assumptions. The individual indicator mappings
\[
g_i(X_t) := \mathbb{I}\{X_t \le s_i\}
\]
are measurable functions of $X_t$ \citep[p. 36]{klenke2005}.
Since measurability preserves strict stationarity, the binarized processes
$(g_i(X_t))$ and $(h_i(X_t))$ are strictly stationary whenever $(X_t)$ is
strictly stationary \citep[p. 157]{kallenberg_2021}.
Consequently, the vector-valued processes $\boldsymbol{Z}_t$ is strictly stationary as well.\\
Moreover, each indicator variable $Z_{t,i}$ is defined on the same probability space
as $X_t$, and the $\sigma$-field it generates satisfies
\[
\sigma(Z_{t,i} : J \le t \le L) \subset \sigma(X_t : J \le t \le L), \qquad J \le L,
\]
which implies $\alpha_{Z_{t,i}}(n) \le \alpha_{X_t}(n)$ for all $i$ and $n$. Hence,
$\boldsymbol{Z}_t$ is also $\alpha$-mixing with exponentially
decaying coefficients.\\
To derive the asymptotic distribution of $\hat{\boldsymbol{f}}$, we
apply the Cramér–Wold theorem \citep[Corollary 4.5]{kallenberg_2021}. 
Specifically, $\hat{\boldsymbol{f}}$ converges in distribution to a 
multivariate normal vector if and only if the univariate central limit theorem holds for all linear combinations of $\boldsymbol{Z}_t$ with nonzero coefficients, that is,
\[
U_t := \boldsymbol{a}^{\top}\boldsymbol{Z}_t, \qquad 
\boldsymbol{a} \in \mathbb{R}^{m}\setminus\{\boldsymbol{0}\}.
\]
To see that $U_t$ is measurable, let
\[
L: \mathbb{R}^{m+1} \rightarrow \mathbb{R}, \quad L(\boldsymbol{z}) = \boldsymbol{a}^{\top}\boldsymbol{z}.
\]
Since $L$ is linear on a finite-dimensional space, it is continuous 
and hence Borel-measurable \citep[Theorem~1.88]{klenke2005}. 
The measurability of $g(X_t) = \boldsymbol{Z}_t$ follows from the 
measurability of its components, and the composition 
$U_t = L \circ g \circ X_t$ is measurable by 
\citet[Theorem~1.80]{klenke2005}. Hence, $U_t$ inherits strict stationarity and $\alpha$-mixing with
$\alpha_{U_t}(n) \le \alpha_{X_t}(n) \le C\rho^n$ from $(X_t)$. We apply the CLT of
\citet[Theorem 7]{Ibragimov1962}, which requires four conditions:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item[\normalfont(i)] \textbf{Strict stationarity.} Verified above, as $U_t$ is
    a measurable function of the strictly stationary process $(X_t)$.
    
    \item[\normalfont(ii)] \textbf{$\alpha$-mixing.} Verified above, with
    $\alpha_{U_t}(n) \le C\rho^n$.
    
    \item[\normalfont(iii)] \textbf{Summability.} The mixing coefficients are required
    to satisfy
    \[
    \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \alpha(n)^{\lambda/(2+\lambda)} < \infty
    \quad \text{for some } \lambda > 0.
    \]
    Under exponential decay $\alpha(n) \le C\rho^n$, this follows from
    \begin{align*}
    \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \alpha(n)^{\lambda/(2+\lambda)}
    &\le C^{\lambda/(2+\lambda)} \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \rho^{\,n\lambda/(2+\lambda)} \\
    &= C^{\lambda/(2+\lambda)} \frac{\rho^{\lambda/(2+\lambda)}}
    {1-\rho^{\lambda/(2+\lambda)}} < \infty,
    \end{align*}
    since $0 < \rho^{\lambda/(2+\lambda)} < 1$ for all $\lambda > 0$.
    
    \item[\normalfont(iv)] \textbf{Moment condition.} The theorem requires
    $\mathrm{E}|U_t|^{2+\lambda} < \infty$ for some $\lambda > 0$. Since each
    $Z_{t,i} \in \{0,1\}$, the projection $U_t$ is bounded by
    $M := \sum_{i=0}^{m-1}|a_i| < \infty$, and hence
    $\mathrm{E}|U_t|^{2+\lambda} \le M^{2+\lambda} < \infty$ for all $\lambda > 0$.
\end{enumerate}
All four conditions being satisfied, Ibragimov's CLT applies to $(U_t)$, and by the 
Cramér--Wold device, the asymptotic variance matrix $\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{\boldsymbol{Z}}$ with elemenets $\sigma_{\boldsymbol{Z};ij}$ is determined by
\[
\boldsymbol{a}^{\top}\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{\boldsymbol{Z}}\boldsymbol{a} = \mathrm{Var}(U_t)
+ 2\sum_{h=1}^{\infty}\mathrm{Cov}(U_t, U_{t+h}),
\]
which gives
\[
\sigma_{\boldsymbol{Z};ij} = \mathrm{Cov}(Z_{t,i}, Z_{t,j})
+ \sum_{h=1}^{\infty}\bigl[\mathrm{Cov}(Z_{t,i}, Z_{t+h,j}) 
+ \mathrm{Cov}(Z_{t,j}, Z_{t+h,i})\bigr].
\]
Using $\mathrm{Cov}(Z_{t,i}, Z_{t,j}) = f_{\min\{i,j\}} - f_i f_j$ and
$\mathrm{Cov}(Z_{t,i}, Z_{t+h,j}) = f_{ij}(h) - f_i f_j$, the elements of
$\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{\boldsymbol{Z}}$ take the explicit form
\[
\sigma_{\boldsymbol{Z};ij} = \bigl(f_{\min\{i,j\}} - f_i f_j\bigr)
+ \sum_{h=1}^{\infty}\bigl(f_{ij}(h) + f_{ji}(h) - 2f_i f_j\bigr).
\]

\begin{theorem}[Asymptotic Normality of $\hat{\boldsymbol{f}}$]\label{thm:clt_fhat}
Let $(X_t)$ be strictly stationary and $\alpha$-mixing with exponentially decaying
coefficients. Then
\[
\sqrt{n}\bigl(\hat{\boldsymbol{f}} - \boldsymbol{f}\bigr)
\xrightarrow[n\to\infty]{d}
\mathcal{N}\bigl(\boldsymbol{0},\, \boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{\boldsymbol{Z}}\bigr),
\]
where $\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{\boldsymbol{Z}} = (\sigma_{\boldsymbol{Z};ij})_{i,j=0,\ldots,m-1}$ is given by
\begin{equation*}\label{eq:sigma_Z}
\sigma_{\boldsymbol{Z};ij} = \bigl(f_{\min\{i,j\}} - f_if_j\bigr)
+ \sum_{h=1}^{\infty}\bigl(f_{ij}(h) + f_{ji}(h) - 2f_if_j\bigr).
\end{equation*}
\end{theorem}
\section{Asymptotic Properties of Sample Dispersion Measures}
We can now use this information of the asymptotic distribution of $\hat{\boldsymbol{f}}$ to compute the asymptotic properties of sample measures that are based on $\hat{\boldsymbol{f}}$. 
According to the multivariate delta method \citep[Chapter~3]{VanderVaart1998}, 
let $(\boldsymbol{\Theta}^*_n)_{n \in \mathbb{N}}$ be a sequence of random vectors 
in $\mathbb{R}^m$ satisfying
\[
    \sqrt{n}(\boldsymbol{\Theta}^*_n - \boldsymbol{\Theta}) 
    \xrightarrow{d} \mathcal{N}(\boldsymbol{0}, \boldsymbol{\Sigma}^*),
\]
and let $g^*\colon \mathbb{R}^m \to \mathbb{R}$ be continuously differentiable 
at $\boldsymbol{\Theta}$. Then
\[
    \sqrt{n}\bigl(g^*(\boldsymbol{\Theta}^*_n) - g^*(\boldsymbol{\Theta})\bigr)
    \xrightarrow{d} \mathcal{N}\bigl(0,\, \boldsymbol{j}\boldsymbol{\Sigma}^*
    \boldsymbol{j}^\top\bigr),
\]
where $\boldsymbol{j}$ is the Jacobian with components 
$j_i = \frac{\partial g^*(\boldsymbol{\Theta})}{\partial \theta_i}$. \\
\subsection*{IOV}
Since we have shown that $\boldsymbol{\hat{f}}$ is asymptotically normal we can set $\boldsymbol{\Theta^*} = \hat{\boldsymbol{f}}$ and use this to derive the asymptotic properties of sample measures like the IOV by setting $g^*: \mathbb{R}^m \rightarrow \mathbb{R}, \quad g^*(\hat{\boldsymbol{f}}) = \mathrm{IOV}(\hat{\boldsymbol{f}})$. The respective Jacobian is given by:
\begin{align*}
\boldsymbol{j}_{\mathrm{IOV}} &= \left(\frac{\partial \mathrm{IOV}(\boldsymbol{f})}{\partial f_0 }, \ldots, \frac{\partial \mathrm{IOV}(\boldsymbol{f})}{\partial f_{m-1} }\right) = \frac{4}{m}\left((1- 2 f_0), \ldots,(1-2f_{m-1}) \right)
\end{align*}
which leads to an asymptotic variance of the IOV:
\begin{align*}
    \sqrt{n}\mathrm{Var}(\widehat{IOV(\boldsymbol{f})}) &\overset{asy.}{=} \boldsymbol{j}_{\mathrm{IOV}} \boldsymbol{\Sigma}_Z \boldsymbol{j}_{\mathrm{IOV}}^{\top} = \sum_{i, ~j =0}^{m-1}  \frac{4(1- 2 f_i)}{m} \frac{4(1- 2 f_j)}{m} \sigma_{\boldsymbol{Z}; ij}\\
    &= \frac{16}{m^2} \sum_{i,j = 0}^{m-1} (1-2f_i)(1- 2 f_j) \sigma_{\boldsymbol{Z}; ij}
\end{align*}
However, since $IOV$ is not a linear function, the first-order delta method only provides information about the asymptotic distribution and variance, not on the finite-sample bias:
\[(\widehat{\mathrm{IOV}} \overset{p}{\rightarrow} \mathrm{IOV}(\boldsymbol{f})) \not\Rightarrow (\mathrm{E}[\widehat{\mathrm{IOV}}] = \mathrm{IOV}(\boldsymbol{f})).\]
For nonlinear transformations, the curvature induces a bias of order $n^{-1}$. Since
$\mathrm{IOV}(\boldsymbol{f}) = \frac{4}{m}\sum_{i=0}^{m-1}f_i(1-f_i)$ is quadratic in $\boldsymbol{f}$, all derivatives of order three and higher are equal to 0. Consequently, the second-order Taylor expansion is not merely an
approximation but exact:
\[
\mathrm{IOV}(\hat{\boldsymbol{f}}) = \mathrm{IOV}(\boldsymbol{f}) 
+ \boldsymbol{j}_{\mathrm{IOV}}(\hat{\boldsymbol{f}} - \boldsymbol{f}) 
+ \frac{1}{2}(\hat{\boldsymbol{f}}-\boldsymbol{f})^\top 
\boldsymbol{H}_{\mathrm{IOV}}(\boldsymbol{f})(\hat{\boldsymbol{f}}-\boldsymbol{f}),
\]
with no remainder term. $\boldsymbol{H}_{\mathrm{IOV}}(\boldsymbol{f})$
is the Hessian matrix such that\[\boldsymbol{H}_{\mathrm{IOV}}(\boldsymbol{f}) = \left(\frac{\partial^2 \mathrm{IOV}(\boldsymbol{f})}{\partial f_i f_j}\right)_{i,j = 0, \ldots, m-1} = \begin{cases}
    -\frac{8}{m} & \text{if } i = j\\
    0 & \text{else}
\end{cases} = -\frac{8}{m} \boldsymbol{I}_m.\]
To take the expectation of the quadratic term, we use the following lemma: 
\begin{lemma}\label{lem:quadform}
Let $\boldsymbol{u} \in \mathbb{R}^m$ be a random vector with
$\mathrm{E}[\boldsymbol{u}] = \boldsymbol{0}$ and let  
$\boldsymbol{A} \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times m}$ be deterministic. Then
\[
\mathrm{E}[\boldsymbol{u}^\top \boldsymbol{A}\boldsymbol{u}] 
= \mathrm{tr}(\boldsymbol{A}\,\mathrm{Cov}(\boldsymbol{u})).
\]
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
Since $\boldsymbol{u}^\top \boldsymbol{A}\boldsymbol{u}$ is a scalar, 
it follows from the cyclic property of the trace 
\citep[Theorem~1.5]{Banerjee2014} that
$\boldsymbol{u}^\top \boldsymbol{A}\boldsymbol{u} 
= \mathrm{tr}(\boldsymbol{A}\boldsymbol{u}\boldsymbol{u}^\top)$.
Because expectation and trace are both linear and 
$\mathrm{E}[\boldsymbol{u}] = \boldsymbol{0}$, it follows that
\[
\mathrm{E}[\boldsymbol{u}^\top \boldsymbol{A}\boldsymbol{u}]
= \mathrm{tr}\bigl(\boldsymbol{A}\,\mathrm{E}[\boldsymbol{u}\boldsymbol{u}^\top]\bigr)
= \mathrm{tr}(\boldsymbol{A}\,\mathrm{Cov}(\boldsymbol{u})). \qedhere
\]
\end{proof}
\noindent Since $\mathrm{E}(\boldsymbol{j}_{\mathrm{IOV}} (\hat{\boldsymbol{f}} - \boldsymbol{f})) = 0$,
the finite-sample bias is given by
\begin{align*}
    \mathrm{Bias}(\mathrm{IOV}(\hat{\boldsymbol{f}})) 
    &= \mathrm{E}\!\left[\frac{1}{2}(\hat{\boldsymbol{f}}- \boldsymbol{f})^\top 
    \boldsymbol{H}_{\mathrm{IOV}}(\boldsymbol{f})(\hat{\boldsymbol{f}}- \boldsymbol{f})\right] \\
    &= \frac{1}{2}\,\mathrm{tr}\!\left(\boldsymbol{H}_{\mathrm{IOV}}\,
    \mathrm{Cov}(\hat{\boldsymbol{f}})\right) \\
    &= \frac{1}{2}\,\mathrm{tr}\!\left(-\frac{8}{m}\boldsymbol{I}_m \cdot 
    \frac{1}{n}\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_Z\right) \\
    &= -\frac{4}{mn} \sum_{i=0}^{m-1} \sigma_{\boldsymbol{Z};\,ii},
\end{align*}
\noindent where we used Lemma~\ref{lem:quadform} in the second equality. The result implies that the bias is strictly negative and only depends on the marginal variances 
$\sigma_{\boldsymbol{Z}; ii}$ of the individual estimators $f_i$,  not on the covariances. 
The results are summarized in the following theorem: \\
\begin{theorem}[Asymptotic Distribution of $\widehat{\mathrm{IOV}}$]\label{thm:iov_asymp}
Under the assumptions of Theorem~\ref{thm:clt_fhat}:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item[\normalfont(i)] \textbf{Asymptotic variance:}
    \begin{equation*}\label{eq:var_iov}
        \sqrt{n}\mathrm{Var}(\widehat{\mathrm{IOV}}) \overset{asy.}{=}
        \frac{16}{m^2}\sum_{i,j=0}^{m-1}(1-2f_i)(1-2f_j)\,\sigma_{\boldsymbol{Z};ij}.
    \end{equation*}
    \item[\normalfont(ii)] \textbf{Bias to order $n^{-1}$:}
    \begin{equation*}\label{eq:bias_iov}
        \mathrm{Bias}(\widehat{\mathrm{IOV}})
        = -\frac{4}{m\,n}\sum_{i=0}^{m-1}\sigma_{\boldsymbol{Z};ii}.
    \end{equation*}
\end{enumerate}
\end{theorem}
\subsection*{Skewness}
Similarly, the asymptotic properties of other dispersion metrics such as the skew
can be analyzed. A simple measure for the skew of an ordinal process is given by:
\[\mathrm{skew}(\boldsymbol{f}) = \frac{2}{m} \left(\sum_{i=0}^{m-1}f_i\right) - 1.\]
This measure captures asymmetry through the average level of the CDF.
A symmetric distribution is taken as a baseline, and deviations from this
baseline reflect asymmetry in the distribution. The measure is scaled to
$[-1,1]$. For a more detailed discussion of skewness measures for ordinal
data, see \citet{klein_doll_2020}. The three extreme cases illustrate its
behavior clearly:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Left-skewed} ($\mathrm{skew} = 1$): all probability mass is 
    concentrated in the lowest category $s_0$, so $f_i = 1$ for all $i$ and
    $\mathrm{skew} = \frac{2}{m} \cdot m - 1 = 1$.
    \item \textbf{Symmetric} ($\mathrm{skew} = 0$): the distribution is symmetric
    around the midpoint, which implies $f_i+ f_{m-1-i} = 1$ for all $i$. It follows that $\sum_{i=0}^{m-1} f_i = m/2$ and
    $\mathrm{skew} = \frac{2}{m} \cdot \frac{m}{2} - 1 = 0$.
    \item \textbf{Right-skewed} ($\mathrm{skew} = -1$): all probability mass is
    concentrated in the highest category $s_m$, so $f_i = 0$ for all $i < m$ and
    $\mathrm{skew} = \frac{2}{m} \cdot 0 - 1 = -1$.
\end{itemize}
The Jacobian for the skew is given by
\[\boldsymbol{j}_{skew} = \left(\frac{\partial ~ \mathrm{skew}(\boldsymbol{f})}{\partial f_0}, \ldots, \frac{\partial ~\mathrm{skew}(\boldsymbol{f})}{\partial f_{m-1}}\right) = \left(\frac{2}{m}, \ldots, \frac{2}{m}\right)\]
such that we end up with the following results:
\begin{proposition}[Asymptotic Properties of $\widehat{\mathrm{skew}}$]\label{prop:skew}
Under the assumptions of Theorem~\ref{thm:clt_fhat}:
\begin{equation*}\label{eq:var_skew}
    \sqrt{n}\mathrm{Var}(\widehat{\mathrm{skew}}) \overset{asy.}{=}
    \frac{4}{m^2}\sum_{i,j=0}^{m-1}\sigma_{Z;ij}.
\end{equation*}
Since $\mathrm{skew}$ is linear in $\boldsymbol{f}$, the Hessian vanishes and the
estimator is exactly unbiased for all $n$.
\end{proposition}
\chapter{Introducing Missingness}\label{chap:missingness}
\section{Missingness Mechanisms}\label{sec:missingness}
In many applied settings, categorical time series are not fully 
observed. Individual time points may be missing due to sensor 
failures, non-response in survey data, or dropout in longitudinal 
studies. Before modelling the statistical consequences of 
missingness, it is useful to distinguish between different 
mechanisms that may give rise to it, as these have fundamentally 
different implications for estimation.\\
The classical taxonomy of missing data mechanisms goes back to 
\citet{Rubin1976} and \citet{little_rubin_2002}, who distinguished 
between three types based on the dependence structure between the 
missingness indicator and the underlying data. Let $\boldsymbol{X}^* 
= (\boldsymbol{X}^{(o)}, \boldsymbol{X}^{(m)})$ denote the complete 
data, partitioned into observed and missing components, and let 
$\boldsymbol{O} = (O_1, \ldots, O_n)^\top$ be the vector of 
missingness indicators. A probability model for the missingness 
mechanism specifies the distribution of $\boldsymbol{O}$ conditional 
on $\boldsymbol{X}^*$. Following \citet[Section~13]{diggle_2002}, 
the three mechanisms are classified as follows:
    
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Missing Completely at Random (MCAR):} 
    $\boldsymbol{O}$ is independent of both the observed and the 
    missing data:
    \[
        f(\boldsymbol{o} \mid \boldsymbol{x}^{(o)}, 
        \boldsymbol{x}^{(m)}) = f(\boldsymbol{o}).
    \]
    \item \textbf{Missing at Random (MAR):} $\boldsymbol{O}$ is 
    independent of the missing data, given the observed data:
    \[
        f(\boldsymbol{o} \mid \boldsymbol{x}^{(o)}, 
        \boldsymbol{x}^{(m)}) = f(\boldsymbol{o} \mid 
        \boldsymbol{x}^{(o)}).
    \]
    \item \textbf{Informative/ Missing Not at Random (MNAR):} The distribution 
    of $\boldsymbol{O}$ depends on the missing data 
    $\boldsymbol{x}^{(m)}$, even after conditioning on the 
    observed data $\boldsymbol{x}^{(o)}$.
\end{enumerate}

\begin{remark}
While this classification was originally developed for multivariate 
cross-sectional data \citep{Rubin1976}, it extends naturally to the 
time series setting. In the sequential context considered here, the 
conditioning on observed data is more naturally expressed through 
the history $\mathcal{F}_{t-1} := \sigma(X_s, O_s : s < t)$, 
leading to the time series formulation of the missingness mechanisms: 
MCAR corresponds to $P(O_t = 1 \mid X_t, \mathcal{F}_{t-1}) = \pi$, 
MAR to $P(O_t = 1 \mid X_t, \mathcal{F}_{t-1}) = P(O_t = 1 \mid 
\mathcal{F}_{t-1})$, and MNAR to the case where this equality fails.
\end{remark}
\noindent Following \citet{weiss2021categorical}, the assumption under which the subsequent results are derived is 
that $(O_t)$ and $(X_t)$ are \emph{independent}, which corresponds 
to the MCAR case. This is the strongest of the three assumptions 
and implies in particular that the corrected estimator 
$\hat{\boldsymbol{f}}^*$ introduced in Section~\ref{sec:Amp Mod} 
is consistent. Under MAR or MNAR, the independence assumption 
fails and estimators may be biased and inconsistent — as 
illustrated later in Section~\ref{sec:mnar}.

\section{Amplitude Modulation}\label{sec:Amp Mod}
\subsection*{Definition of the observation process}
To model the missingness, the concept of \textbf{amplitude modulation} --
originally introduced by \citet{Parzen1963} in the context of spectral
analysis of time series with missing observations, and adapted to the
categorical setting by \citet{weiss2021categorical} -- is employed. This approach models missingness through a binary stochastic process $(O_t)_t$ that runs concurrently with the main process $(X_t)_t$ and modulates its "amplitude"  in a binary manner:
\[
O_t \in \{0,1\} \quad \forall t
\]
where $O_t = 1$ indicates that $X_t$ is fully observed (amplitude = 1), and $O_t = 0$ indicates that $X_t$ is missing (amplitude = 0).\\
The amplitude modulating process $O_t$ can be:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Deterministic}: For each $t$, $O_t = o_t$ where $o_t \in \{0,1\}$ is a known, fixed value.
    \item \textbf{Stochastic}: $O_t$ follows a probability distribution with $\pi_t := P(O_t=1)$, possibly dependent on the history of $X_t$, $O_t$, or external factors.
\end{itemize}
\subsection*{Bias of the naive estimator}
The amplitude modulation is applied to the binarization vector $\boldsymbol{Z}_t$ to obtain the observable process $(O_t \boldsymbol{Z}_t)$. 
This representation replaces missing observations by zeros and therefore yields a fully observable binary process. Although information is lost whenever $O_t=0$, 
the resulting process can be analyzed using standard tools for stationary stochastic processes.
We then define the naive estimator of $\boldsymbol{f}$ as:
\[\hat{\boldsymbol{\mathfrak{f}}}:= \frac{1}{n} \sum_{t=1}^{n}O_t \boldsymbol{Z}_t.\]
To assess the performance of this naive estimator we compute its expectation
\[\mathrm{E}[\hat{\boldsymbol{\mathfrak{f}}}] = \frac{1}{n} \sum_{t = 1}^{n} \mathrm{E}[O_t\boldsymbol{Z_t}] = \frac{1}{n} \sum_{t = 1}^{n} \mathrm{E}[\boldsymbol{Z_t} \mathrm{E}[O_t|\boldsymbol{Z}_t]],\]
where the last equality follows from the law of iterated expectations.\\
The dependence structure between $(O_t)_t$ and $(\boldsymbol{Z}_t)_t$ (or equivalently $(X_t)_t$) is crucial. Under the assumption of independence (see Section~\ref{sec:missingness}, part MCAR) we have $\mathrm{E}[O_t|\boldsymbol{Z}_t] = \mathrm{E}[O_t]$ and the expression simplifies to
\[\frac{1}{n} \sum_{t = 1}^{n} \mathrm{E}[\boldsymbol{Z_t} \mathrm{E}[O_t|\boldsymbol{Z}_t]] = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{t=1}^{n} \mathrm{E}[\boldsymbol{Z}_t]  \mathrm{E}[O_t] = \left(\frac{1}{n} \sum_{t=1}^{n} \mathrm{E}[O_t]\right) \boldsymbol{f} \]
\subsection*{The missingness corrected estimator}
\begin{definition}[Corrected Estimator of $\boldsymbol{f}$]\label{def:correctedest}
Since $\mathrm{E}[\hat{\boldsymbol{\mathfrak{f}}}]$ deviates from $\boldsymbol{f}$
by the factor $\frac{1}{n}\sum_{t=1}^{n}\mathrm{E}[O_t]$, the corrected estimator, as proposed by \citet{weiss2021categorical}, 
of $\boldsymbol{f}$ is defined as
\[
    \hat{\boldsymbol{f}}^*
    := \frac{\frac{1}{n} \sum_{t=1}^{n}O_t \boldsymbol{Z}_t}
            {\frac{1}{n}\sum_{t=1}^{n} O_t}
    =: \frac{\hat{\boldsymbol{\mathfrak{f}}}}{\bar{O}}.
\]\end{definition}
\noindent Under the assumption of the independence of $(O_t)$ and $(X_t)$, the stochastic properties of the corrected estimator $\hat{\boldsymbol{f^*}}$ can be studied. If $(O_t)$ is deterministic, then $\mathrm{E}[O_t] = o_t \in \{0,1\}$ for any given $t$. It follows that
\[\frac{1}{n}\sum_{t=1}^{n} \mathrm{E}[O_t] = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{t=1}^{n} O_t = \bar{O},\]
and therefore, here $\hat{\boldsymbol{f}^*} = \hat{\boldsymbol{\mathfrak{f}}}/\bar{O}$ is a perfectly unbiased estimator of $\boldsymbol{f}$. However, if $(O_t)$ is a stochastic process then $\mathrm{E}[O_t] = \pi_t \in (0; 1]$ and
\[\frac{1}{n}\sum_{t=1}^{n} \mathrm{E}[O_t]= \frac{1}{n}\sum_{t=1}^{n} \pi_t \neq \frac{1}{n}\sum_{t=1}^{n} O_t = \bar{O},\]
to where $\hat{\boldsymbol{f^*}}$ is generally biased.
\section{Asymptotic Theory for the corrected estimator} \label{sec:fstar_hat}
\subsection*{The extended process - stationarity and $\alpha$-mixing}
To study this bias we assume $(O_t)$ and $(X_t)$ to be strictly stationary and 
$\alpha$-mixing with exponentially decaying coefficients (see Definition~\ref{def:mixing}), 
with $\mathrm{E}[O_t] = \pi $, an autocovariance function $\gamma_{O}(h) := \mathrm{Cov}[O_h, O_0]$ 
and joint expectations $\pi(h):= \mathrm{E}[O_h O_0] = \gamma_O(h) + \pi^2$. To study 
the joint asymptotic distribution of the estimator $\hat{\boldsymbol{f}}^*$, we 
introduce the extended binary vector $\boldsymbol{Z}^*_t$ in order to derive the joint asymptotic distribution of all sample averages entering its definition:
\[\boldsymbol{Z}^*_t:= (O_t, O_t Z_{t,0}, \ldots, O_t Z_{t, m-1})^\top =  O_t (1, \boldsymbol{Z}^\top_t)^\top,\]
where the elements of $\boldsymbol{Z}_t^*$ are numbered as $Z_{t,-1}^*, Z_{t,0}^*, \ldots, Z_{t, m-1}^*$. The expectation of $\boldsymbol{Z}_t^*$ is given by
\begin{align*}
    \mathrm{E}[\boldsymbol{Z}^{*}_t] &= (\mathrm{E}[O_t], \mathrm{E}[O_t Z_{t,0}], \ldots, \mathrm{E}[O_t Z_{t, m-1}])^\top \\
    &= (\mathrm{E}[O_t], \mathrm{E}[O_t] \mathrm{E}[Z_{t, 0}], \ldots, \mathrm{E}[O_t] \mathrm{E}[Z_{t, m-1}])^\top \tag{Independence}\\
    &= (\pi, \pi f_0, \ldots, \pi f_{m-1})^\top \\
    &=\pi (1, \boldsymbol{f}^{\top})^\top =: \boldsymbol{\mu}_{\boldsymbol{Z}_t^{*}}.
\end{align*}
Since the processes $(O_t)$ and $(\boldsymbol{Z}_t)$ are independent and stationary, the joint process $((O_t, \boldsymbol{Z}_t)^{\top})$ is also stationary. Moreover, if both processes are $\alpha$-mixing, then the joint process is $\alpha$-mixing. As $\boldsymbol{Z}_t^*$ is a measurable function of $(O_t, \boldsymbol{Z})^\top$ it follows that $\boldsymbol{Z}^*_t$ is stationary and $\alpha$-mixing (with exponentially decaying coefficients).
\subsection*{Multivariate CLT}

\begin{proposition}[Asymptotic distribution of $\bar{\boldsymbol{Z}}^*_n$]
\label{prop:clt_zstar}
Let $(X_t)$ and $(O_t)$ be independent, strictly stationary and $\alpha$-mixing 
with exponentially decaying coefficients, with $\mathrm{E}[O_t] = \pi \in (0,1]$. 
Define the extended process
\[
    \boldsymbol{Z}^*_t := O_t(1, \boldsymbol{Z}_t^\top)^\top \in \mathbb{R}^{m+1},
\]
with sample mean $\bar{\boldsymbol{Z}}^*_n := \frac{1}{n}\sum_{t=1}^{n} 
\boldsymbol{Z}^*_t$. Then
\[
    \sqrt{n}\!\left(\bar{\boldsymbol{Z}}^*_n 
    - \boldsymbol{\mu}_{\boldsymbol{Z}^*}\right) 
    \xrightarrow{d} \mathcal{N}\!\left(\boldsymbol{0},\, 
    \boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{\boldsymbol{Z}^*}\right),
\]
where $\boldsymbol{\mu}_{\boldsymbol{Z}^*} = \pi(1, \boldsymbol{f}^\top)^\top$ 
and 
\[
    \boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{\boldsymbol{Z}^*} = \mathrm{Var}(\boldsymbol{Z}^*_t) 
    + 2\sum_{h=1}^{\infty}\mathrm{Cov}(\boldsymbol{Z}^*_t, \boldsymbol{Z}^*_{t+h}),
\]
with elements $\sigma_{\boldsymbol{Z}^*;\,i,j}$ as given in 
cases \textnormal{(i)--(iii)} below.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Since $U_t^* = \boldsymbol{a}^\top \boldsymbol{Z}_t^*$ is a measurable function 
of the strictly stationary and $\alpha$-mixing process $\boldsymbol{Z}_t^*$, it 
inherits both properties, with $\alpha_{U_t^*}(n) \leq \alpha_{\boldsymbol{Z}_t^*}(n) 
\leq C\rho^n$ following from the inclusion $\sigma(U_t^*) \subset \sigma(\boldsymbol{Z}_t^*)$. 
The summability condition therefore holds by the same geometric series argument as 
in Theorem~\ref{thm:clt_fhat}. Since each $Z_{t,i}^* \in \{0,1\}$, the projection $U_t^*$ 
is bounded and all moments are finite. Ibragimov's CLT \citep[Theorem~7]{Ibragimov1962} 
therefore applies to $U_t^*$, and the Cramér--Wold theorem 
\citep[Corollary~4.5]{kallenberg_2021} yields the multivariate result.
\end{proof}
\begin{remark}[Elements of $\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{\boldsymbol{Z}^*}$]
\label{rem:sigma_zstar}
\noindent Note that since $O_t \in \{0,1\}$ with $\mathrm{P}(O_t = 1) = \pi$, 
it follows that $O_t \sim \mathrm{Bernoulli}(\pi)$, so that $\mathrm{E}[O_t] = \pi$, 
$\mathrm{Var}(O_t) = \pi(1-\pi)$, and $\mathrm{E}[O_t^2] = \pi$. Recall also 
that $\mathrm{E}[O_t O_{t+h}] = \pi(h) = \gamma_O(h) + \pi^2$ for all 
$h \in \mathbb{N}$. The individual elements of $\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{\boldsymbol{Z}^*}$ 
are then given by \phantomsection \label{cal:Sigma_Z}
\begin{align*}
    &\textbf{(i)}\ i = j = -1: \\
    &\quad \mathrm{Var}(O_t) + 2\sum_{h=1}^\infty \mathrm{Cov}(O_t, O_{t+h}) \\
    &\quad= \pi(1-\pi) + 2\sum_{h=1}^{\infty}\gamma_O(h) =: \sigma_{\boldsymbol{Z}^*;\,-1-1} \\[2ex]
    &\textbf{(ii)}\ j > i = -1: \\
    &\quad \mathrm{Cov}(O_t, O_t Z_{t,j}) 
    + 2\sum_{h=1}^\infty \mathrm{Cov}(O_t, O_{t+h}Z_{t+h,j}) \\
    &\quad = f_j\,\sigma_{\boldsymbol{Z}^*;\,-1-1} \\[2ex]
    &\textbf{(iii)}\ i,j \geq 0: \\
    &\quad \mathrm{Cov}(O_t Z_{t,i}, O_tZ_{t,j}) 
    + \sum_{h=1}^{\infty}\bigl[\mathrm{Cov}(O_t Z_{t,i}, O_{t+h}Z_{t+h,j}) 
    + \mathrm{Cov}(O_{t+h}Z_{t+h, i}, O_t Z_{t,j})\bigr] \\
    &\quad = f_i f_j\,\sigma_{\boldsymbol{Z}^*;\,-1-1} 
    + \pi(f_{\min \{i,j\}} - f_i f_j)  
    + \sum_{h=1}^{\infty} \pi(h)(f_{ij}(h) + f_{ji}(h) - 2f_i f_j)
\end{align*}\noindent
The detailed derivations of these expressions are provided in Appendix \ref{app:derivation_covarianceZ}.
\end{remark}
\noindent To now derive the asymptotic properties of $\hat{\boldsymbol{f}}^*$ we define the vector-valued function $\boldsymbol{g}:= [0,1]^{m+1} \rightarrow [0,1]^{m}$ as
\[g_j(x_{-1}, x_{0}, \ldots, x_{m-1}) = x_j / x_{-1}, \quad \text{for } j = 0 \ldots, m-1,\]
such that
\begin{align*}
    \boldsymbol{g}(\bar{\boldsymbol{Z}^*_t}) &= (g_0(\bar{\boldsymbol{Z}}^*_t), \ldots, g_{m-1}(\bar{\boldsymbol{Z}}^*_t))^\top  =  (\hat{f}_0 O_t/O_t, \ldots, \hat{f}_{m-1} O_t/O_t)^\top\\
    &= \hat{\boldsymbol{f}}^*
\end{align*}
and  accordingly
\begin{align*}
    g(\boldsymbol{\mu}_{\boldsymbol{Z}^*}) = \boldsymbol{f}.
\end{align*}
The Jacobian of $\boldsymbol{g}(\boldsymbol{x})$ evaluated at $\boldsymbol{x} = \boldsymbol{\mu}_{\boldsymbol{Z}^*}$ is given by
\[\boldsymbol{G}_{m\times (m+1)} =
\begin{pmatrix}
    \frac{\partial g_0(\boldsymbol{\mu}_{\boldsymbol{Z}^*})}{\partial x_{-1}}& \ldots & \frac{\partial g_0(\boldsymbol{\mu}_{\boldsymbol{Z}^*})}{\partial x_{m-1}}\\
    \vdots & \cdots& \vdots\\
    \frac{\partial g_{m-1}(\boldsymbol{\boldsymbol{\mu}_{\boldsymbol{Z}^*}})}{\partial x_{-1}}& \ldots & \frac{\partial g_{m-1}(\boldsymbol{\mu}_{\boldsymbol{Z}^*})}{\partial x_{m-1}}
\end{pmatrix} =
\begin{pmatrix}
    -\frac{f_0}{\pi} &  \frac{1}{\pi} & 0 & \ldots & 0\\
    -\frac{f_1}{\pi} & 0 & \frac{1}{\pi}&\ddots & \vdots \\
    \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \ddots & 0\\
    -\frac{f_{m-1}}{\pi}& 0 &\ldots& 0 & \frac{1}{\pi}
\end{pmatrix}\]
Then, following the delta method, we have the following result that corresponds to Theorem~1 in \citet{weiss2021categorical}: 
\begin{theorem}[Asymptotic Normality of $\hat{\boldsymbol{f}}^*$]\label{thm:clt_fstarhat}
Let $(X_t)$ be a strictly stationary, ordinal process that is $\alpha$-mixing with exponentially decaying
coefficients. Let the modulating process $(O_t)$ also be strictly stationary and $\alpha$-mixing with exponentially decaying coefficients, with moments $\mathrm{E}[O_t] = \pi > 0$ and $\mathrm{E}[O_h O_0] = \pi(h) > 0$. Furthermore, let $(O_t)$ and $(X_t)$ be independent, where $X_t$ is observed if $O_t = 1$. Then the corrected estimator from Definition~\ref{def:correctedest} satisfies the following multivariate CLT:
\begin{equation*}\label{eq:clt_fstarhat}
    \sqrt{n}\bigl(\hat{\boldsymbol{f}}^* - \boldsymbol{f}\bigr)
    \xrightarrow[n\to\infty]{d}
    \mathcal{N}\bigl(\boldsymbol{0}, \boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{\hat{\boldsymbol{f}}^*}\bigr),
    \quad \text{with } \boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{\hat{\boldsymbol{f}}^*} = (\sigma_{\hat{\boldsymbol{f}}^*;ij})_{i,j=0,\ldots,m-1},
\end{equation*}
where
\begin{equation*}\label{eq:sigma_fstar}
    \sigma_{\hat{\boldsymbol{f}}^*;i,j} = \frac{1}{\pi} (f_{\min \{i,j\}}- f_if_j) + \frac{1}{\pi^2} \sum_{h=1}^{\infty}\pi(h)(f_{ij}(h)+f_{ji}(h)-2f_if_j).
\end{equation*}\end{theorem}
\noindent The structure of $\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{\hat{\boldsymbol{f}^*}}$ can be computed as follows
\begin{align*}
    \boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{\hat{\boldsymbol{f}^*}} &= \boldsymbol{G} \boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{\boldsymbol{Z}^*} \boldsymbol{G}^\top \\
    &= \begin{pmatrix}
    -\frac{f_0}{\pi} &  \frac{1}{\pi} & 0 & \ldots & 0\\
    -\frac{f_1}{\pi} & 0 & \frac{1}{\pi}&\ddots & \vdots \\
    \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \ddots & 0\\
    -\frac{f_{m-1}}{\pi}& 0 &\ldots& 0 & \frac{1}{\pi}
    \end{pmatrix}
    \begin{pmatrix}
        \sigma_{\boldsymbol{Z}^*; -1, -1}& \ldots & \sigma_{\boldsymbol{Z}^*; -1, m-1}\\
        \vdots & \ddots &\vdots \\
        \sigma_{\boldsymbol{Z}^*; m-1, -1} & \ldots & \sigma_{\boldsymbol{Z}^*; m-1, m-1}
    \end{pmatrix}
    \boldsymbol{G}^\top\\
    &= \begin{pmatrix}
        -\frac{f_0}{\pi}\sigma_{\boldsymbol{Z}^*; -1,-1} + \frac{1}{\pi}\sigma_{\boldsymbol{Z}^*; 0, -1} &\ldots&-\frac{f_0}{\pi}\sigma_{\boldsymbol{Z}^*; -1, m-1} + \frac{1}{\pi}\sigma_{\boldsymbol{Z}^*; 0, m-1} \\
        \vdots & \ddots &\vdots \\
        -\frac{f_{m-1}}{\pi}\sigma_{\boldsymbol{Z}^*; -1, -1} + \frac{1}{\pi}\sigma_{\boldsymbol{Z}^*; m-1, -1} &\ldots&-\frac{f_{m-1}}{\pi}\sigma_{\boldsymbol{Z}^*; -1, m-1} + \frac{1}{\pi}\sigma_{\boldsymbol{Z}^*; m-1, m-1} \\
    \end{pmatrix}\boldsymbol{G}^\top.
\end{align*}
\noindent Such that the individual elements look like this:
\begin{align*}
    \sigma_{\hat{\boldsymbol{f}}^*; i, j}  &= (-\frac{f_j}{\pi}) (-\frac{f_i}{\pi}\sigma_{\boldsymbol{Z}^*; -1, -1} + \frac{1}{\pi} \sigma_{\boldsymbol{Z}^*; i, -1}) + \frac{1}{\pi}(-\frac{f_{i}}{\pi}\sigma_{\boldsymbol{Z}^*; -1, j}+ \frac{1}{\pi} \sigma_{\boldsymbol{Z}^*; i, j}) \\
    &= \frac{f_j f_i }{\pi^2} \sigma_{\boldsymbol{Z}^*; -1, -1} - \frac{f_j}{\pi^2} \sigma_{\boldsymbol{Z}^*; i, -1} - \frac{f_i}{\pi^2}\sigma_{\boldsymbol{Z}^*; -1, j} + \frac{1}{\pi^2} \sigma_{\boldsymbol{Z}^*; i, j} \\
    &= \frac{1}{\pi^2}\left(f_if_j\sigma_{\boldsymbol{Z}^*; -1, -1} - f_if_j\sigma_{\boldsymbol{Z}^*; -1, -1} - f_if_j\sigma_{\boldsymbol{Z}^*; -1, -1} + \sigma_{\boldsymbol{Z}^*; i, j}\right) \\
    &= \frac{1}{\pi^2}(\sigma_{\boldsymbol{Z}^*; i, j}-f_if_j \sigma_{\boldsymbol{Z}^*; -1, -1}) \\
    &= \frac{1}{\pi^2}\biggl(\left[f_if_j\sigma_{\boldsymbol{Z}^*; -1, -1} + \pi(f_{\min \{i,j\}}- f_if_j) + \sum_{h=1}^{\infty}\pi(h)f_{ij}(h)+f_{ji}(h)-2f_if_j\right] \\
    &\quad - f_if_j \sigma_{\boldsymbol{Z}^*; -1, -1}\biggr) \\
    &= \underbrace{\frac{1}{\pi} (f_{\min \{i,j\}}- f_if_j) }_{\mathrm{lag}(0)}+ \frac{1}{\pi^2}\sum_{h=1}^{\infty}\underbrace{\pi(h)(f_{ij}(h)+f_{ji}(h)-2f_if_j)}_{\mathrm{lag}(h)}
\end{align*}
Here, the lag-0 term captures the contemporaneous covariance structure of the
observed process, while the lag-$h$ terms accumulate the serial dependence
across all lags $h \geq 1$. While the asymptotic normality of $\hat{\boldsymbol{f}}^*$
is also established by \citet{weiss2021categorical}, the derivations above make the underlying 
mixing conditions and the Cramér–Wold argument fully explicit.
\subsection*{Bias analysis}
Since $g_j$ is not a linear function of $\boldsymbol{Z}_t^*$, the bias of order
$O(n^{-1})$ is computable using a second-order Taylor expansion. The Hessian of $g_j$ evaluated in $\boldsymbol{\mu}_{\boldsymbol{Z}^*}$ is given by
\[
\boldsymbol{H}_{g_j} = \left(\frac{\partial^2 g_j(\boldsymbol{\mu_{Z^*}})}{\partial x_i \partial x_k}\right)_{i,k=-1,\ldots,m-1} =
\begin{cases}
    \frac{2f_j}{\pi^2}  & \text{if } i = k = -1 \\
    -\frac{1}{\pi^2}    & \text{if } i = -1,\ k = j \\
    0                   & \text{else.}
\end{cases}
\]
Using the symmetry $\sigma_{\boldsymbol{Z}^*;-1j} = \sigma_{\boldsymbol{Z}^*;j(-1)}$ and the equality from Lemma~\ref{lem:quadform} the bias simplifies to
\begin{align*}
    \mathrm{Bias}(\hat{f}_j)
    &= \frac{1}{2}\mathrm{tr}(\boldsymbol{H}_{g_j}\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{\boldsymbol{Z}^*}) \\
    &= \frac{1}{2}h_{g_j;-1-1}\,\sigma_{\boldsymbol{Z}^*;-1-1}
        + h_{g_j;-1j}\,\sigma_{\boldsymbol{Z}^*;-1,j} \\
    &= \frac{1}{2}\cdot\frac{2f_j}{\pi^2}\,\sigma_{\boldsymbol{Z}^*;-1-1}
        - \frac{1}{\pi^2}\cdot f_j\,\sigma_{\boldsymbol{Z}^*;-1-1} \\
    &= \frac{f_j}{\pi^2}\,\sigma_{\boldsymbol{Z}^*;-1-1}
        - \frac{f_j}{\pi^2}\,\sigma_{\boldsymbol{Z}^*;-1-1} = 0,
\end{align*}
where $h_{g_j;ik} = \frac{\partial g_j(\boldsymbol{\mu_{Z^*}})}{\partial x_i \partial x_k}$, and where the last step uses $\sigma_{\boldsymbol{Z}^*;-1j} = f_j\,\sigma_{\boldsymbol{Z}^*;-1-1}$ 
from case~\textbf{(ii)} of \hyperref[rem:sigma_zstar]{$\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{\boldsymbol{Z}^*}$}.\\
\subsection*{Special Cases}
To further study the asymptotic distribution of $\hat{\boldsymbol{f}}^*$,
we consider three special cases of the general result:

\begin{corollary}[Full Observation] \label{cor:Full Obs}
If the process is fully observed, i.e.\ $\pi = 1$, then
\[
    \sigma_{\hat{\boldsymbol{f}}^*;\, i,j}
    = \bigl(f_{\min\{i,j\}} - f_if_j\bigr)
    + \sum_{h=1}^{\infty} f_{ij}(h) + f_{ji}(h) - 2f_if_j
    = \sigma_{\boldsymbol{Z};\, i,j}.
\]
\end{corollary}
\noindent That is, the asymptotic covariance of $\hat{\boldsymbol{f}}^*$ reduces to that of
the complete-data estimator \hyperref[thm:clt_fhat]{$\hat{\boldsymbol{f}}$}, as one would expect.

\begin{corollary}[i.i.d.\ Observation Process]\label{cor:iid_Obs}
If $(O_t)$ is i.i.d., then $\pi(h) = \pi^2$ for all $h \geq 1$,
and the asymptotic covariance simplifies to
\[
    \sigma_{\hat{\boldsymbol{f}}^*;\, i,j}
    = \frac{1}{\pi}\bigl(f_{\min\{i,j\}} - f_if_j\bigr)
    + \sum_{h=1}^{\infty} f_{ij}(h) + f_{ji}(h) - 2f_if_j.
\]
\end{corollary}
\noindent Compared to the complete-data case, the lag-0 term is inflated by a factor of
$1/\pi \geq 1$, reflecting the additional uncertainty introduced by the random
missingness. The lag-$h$ terms for $h \geq 1$, however, are unaffected, since
i.i.d.\ missingness introduces no additional serial dependence into the observation
process.

\begin{corollary}[i.i.d.\ Main Process]\label{cor:iid_Main}
If $(X_t)$ is i.i.d., then $f_{ij}(h) = f_if_j$ for all
$i,j = 0,\ldots,m-1$ and $h \geq 1$, so that the asymptotic
covariance reduces to
\[
    \sigma_{\hat{\boldsymbol{f}}^*;\, i,j}
    = \frac{1}{\pi}\bigl(f_{\min\{i,j\}} - f_i f_j\bigr)
    + \frac{1}{\pi^2}\sum_{h=1}^{\infty} \pi(h)
    \overbrace{(f_{ij}(h) + f_{ji}(h) - 2f_i f_j)}^{=0}
    = \frac{1}{\pi}\bigl(f_{\min\{i,j\}} - f_i f_j\bigr).
\]
\end{corollary}
\noindent When the main process is serially independent, all temporal dependence in
$\hat{\boldsymbol{f}}^*$ vanishes and the asymptotic covariance only depends on the lag-$0$ term. 

\section{Asymptotic marginal properties}
The asymptotic properties of marginal statistics based on $\hat{\boldsymbol{f}}^*$
follow directly from the delta method, in exact analogy to the complete-data case.
The following results correspond to Theorem 2 in \citet{weiss2021categorical}. For clarity, we 
present the asymptotic properties of the IOV and the skew separately, although both 
follow from the same application of the delta method: 
\begin{corollary}[Asymptotic distribution of $\mathrm{IOV}(\hat{\boldsymbol{f}}^*)$]\label{cor:iov_missing}
Under the conditions of Theorem~\ref{thm:clt_fstarhat}, the delta method argument from Theorem~\ref{thm:iov_asymp} 
carries over to the missing-data case with $\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_Z$ replaced by
$\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{\hat{\boldsymbol{f}}^*}$, yielding
\[
    \sqrt{n}\mathrm{Var}\bigl(\mathrm{IOV}(\hat{\boldsymbol{f}}^*)\bigr)
    \overset{\mathrm{asy.}}{=}
    \frac{16}{m^2} \sum_{i,j=0}^{m-1}(1-2f_i)(1-2f_j)\,\sigma_{\hat{\boldsymbol{f}}^*;\, ij}.
\]
Moreover, since $\boldsymbol{H}_{\mathrm{IOV}} = -\frac{8}{m}\boldsymbol{I}_m$ remains
diagonal, the bias to order $n^{-1}$ depends only on the marginal variances of
$\hat{\boldsymbol{f}}^*$:
\[
    \mathrm{Bias}\bigl(\mathrm{IOV}(\hat{\boldsymbol{f}}^*)\bigr)
    = -\frac{4}{mn}\sum_{i=0}^{m-1}\sigma_{\hat{\boldsymbol{f}}^*;\, ii}.
\]
\end{corollary} 
\begin{remark}[Skew under missingness]
The analogous result for the skew follows immediately, since the skew is linear in
$\boldsymbol{f}$ and hence $\boldsymbol{H}_{\mathrm{skew}} = 0$. In particular, the
estimator is unbiased, and its asymptotic variance is given by
\[
    \sqrt{n}\mathrm{Var}\bigl(\mathrm{skew}(\hat{\boldsymbol{f}}^*)\bigr)
    \overset{\mathrm{asy.}}{=}
    \frac{4}{m^2}\sum_{i,j=0}^{m-1}\sigma_{\hat{\boldsymbol{f}}^*;\, ij}.
\]
\end{remark}

\section{Serial Dependence}
\label{sec:serial-dependence}

So far, we have only examined aspects of the marginal distribution of 
the categorical process $(X_t)$. However, an essential component of 
time series analysis is the study of serial dependence. Recall that 
the most widely used measures to describe such dependence are the 
ordinal and nominal Cohen's $\kappa$ (Definition~\ref{def:cohens-kappa}). 
To estimate these measures, we again rely on relative frequencies:
\[
    \hat{\kappa}_{\mathrm{ord}}(h) 
    = \frac{\sum_{j=0}^{m-1}\left(\hat{f}_{jj}^*(h) 
    - \bigl(\hat{f}^*_j\bigr)^2\right)}{\sum_{i=0}^{m-1}
    \hat{f}^*_i\bigl(1-\hat{f}_i^*\bigr)}.
\]
Hence, it remains to define the estimator $\hat{f}^*_{ij}(h)$ for the 
bivariate lag-$h$ probabilities $f_{ij}(h)$. As before, we begin with 
the naive estimator given by the bivariate relative frequency of the 
amplitude-modulated binarizations,
\[
    \hat{\mathfrak{f}}_{ij}(h) 
    = \frac{1}{n-h}\sum_{t=h+1}^{n} O_t\, O_{t-h}\, Z_{t,i}\, Z_{t-h,j},
\]
and compute its expectation:
\begin{align*}
    \mathrm{E}\!\left[\hat{\mathfrak{f}}_{ij}(h)\right]
    &= \frac{1}{n-h}\sum_{t=h+1}^{n}
       \mathrm{E}\!\left[O_t O_{t-h} Z_{t,i} Z_{t-h,j}\right] \\
    &= \frac{1}{n-h}\sum_{t=h+1}^{n}
       \mathrm{E}\!\left[O_t O_{t-h}\right] \cdot 
       \mathrm{E}\!\left[Z_{t,i} Z_{t-h,j}\right] \\
    &= \left(\frac{1}{n-h}\sum_{t=h+1}^{n}
       \mathrm{E}\!\left[O_t O_{t-h}\right]\right) f_{ij}(h),
\end{align*}
where the second equality follows from the independence of $(O_t)$ 
and $(X_t)$, and the third from the stationarity of $(X_t)$. This 
motivates the corrected estimator
\begin{align}
    \hat{f}^*_{ij}(h)
    := \frac{\dfrac{1}{n-h}\displaystyle\sum_{t=h+1}^{n} 
      O_t\, O_{t-h}\, Z_{t,i}\, Z_{t-h,j}}
      {\dfrac{1}{n-h}\displaystyle\sum_{t=h+1}^{n} O_t\, O_{t-h}}
    =: \hat{\mathfrak{f}}_{ij}(h) \big/ \overline{OO}_{\,h}, \label{def:biv_probs_est}
\end{align}
which is also proposed by \citet[p.~4683]{weiss2021categorical}. As in Section~\ref{sec:Amp Mod}, the properties of this estimator 
depend on whether $(O_t)$ is deterministic or stochastic. If $(O_t)$ 
is deterministic, the corrected estimator is exactly unbiased. In the 
stochastic case, both numerator and denominator are random and depend 
on the same process $(O_t)$, so that in general
\[
    \mathrm{E}\!\left[\frac{\hat{\mathfrak{f}}_{ij}(h)}
    {\overline{OO}_{\,h}}\right] \neq 
    \frac{\mathrm{E}\!\left[\hat{\mathfrak{f}}_{ij}(h)\right]}
    {\mathrm{E}\!\left[\overline{OO}_{\,h}\right]},
\]
and hence $\hat{f}^*_{ij}(h)$ is generally biased.

\subsection{Asymptotic distribution under the general model}
\label{subsec:serial-general}

To derive the asymptotic distribution of $\hat{f}^*_{ij}(h)$, we 
proceed analogously to Section~\ref{sec:Amp Mod} and apply the 
Cramér--Wold device to the sample mean of a suitably defined 
binary vector:
\begin{align}
    \boldsymbol{Z}^{(h)*}_t 
    &= \left(
        Z_{t,-2}^{(h)*},\;
        Z_{t, -1}^{(h)*},\;
        \overbrace{\ldots,\, Z_{t,i}^{(h)*},\, \ldots}^{i=0,\,\ldots,\,m-1},\;
        \overbrace{\ldots,\, Z_{t,m+j}^{(h)*},\, \ldots}^{j=0,\,\ldots,\,m-1}
       \right)^\top \\
    &:= \left(
        O_t O_{t-h},\;
        \underbrace{O_t,\, \ldots,\, O_{t} Z_{t,i},\, \ldots}_{=\,\boldsymbol{Z}^*_t},\;
        \ldots,\, O_t O_{t-h} Z_{t,j} Z_{t-h,j},\, \ldots
        \right)^\top.\label{eq:Z^h}
\end{align}
The components of $\boldsymbol{Z}^{(h)*}_t$ are indexed from $-2$ to 
$2m-1$: the component at index $-2$ captures the product $O_tO_{t-h}$, 
the components at indices $-1$ through $m-1$ coincide with 
$\boldsymbol{Z}_t^*$ from Section~\ref{sec:Amp Mod}, and the remaining 
components at indices $m$ through $2m-1$ capture the lag-$h$ interaction 
terms $O_t O_{t-h}Z_{t,i}Z_{t-h,i}$. Since $\kappa_{\mathrm{ord}}$ 
depends only on the diagonal frequencies $\hat{f}^*_{ii}(h)$, it 
suffices to include the corresponding diagonal components in 
$\boldsymbol{Z}_t^{(h)*}$. Should one wish to study a dependence 
measure that also involves off-diagonal frequencies $\hat{f}^*_{ij}(h)$ 
with $i \neq j$, the vector can straightforwardly be extended by the 
additional components $O_t O_{t-h} Z_{t,i} Z_{t-h,j}$.\\
\begin{proposition}[Asymptotic distribution of $\bar{\boldsymbol{Z}}^{(h)*}_n$]\label{prop:clt_zstar_h}
Let the assumptions of Theorem~\ref{thm:clt_fstarhat} hold. Then
\[
    \sqrt{n}\!\left(\bar{\boldsymbol{Z}}^{(h)*}_n - 
    \boldsymbol{\mu}_{\boldsymbol{Z}^{(h)*}}\right) 
    \xrightarrow{d} \mathcal{N}\!\left(\boldsymbol{0},\, 
    \boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{\boldsymbol{Z}^{(h)*}}\right),
\]
where $\bar{\boldsymbol{Z}}^{(h)*}_n := \frac{1}{n}\sum_{t=1}^{n} 
\boldsymbol{Z}^{(h)*}_t$,
\[
    \boldsymbol{\mu}_{\boldsymbol{Z}^{(h)*}} 
    = \bigl(\pi(h),\, \pi,\, \pi\boldsymbol{f}^\top,\, 
      \pi(h)f_{00}(h),\, \ldots,\, 
      \pi(h)f_{m-1m-1}(h)\bigr)^\top,
\]
and $\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{\boldsymbol{Z}^{(h)*}}$ has elements
\[
    \sigma^{(h)}_{ij} = \mathrm{Cov}(Z^{(h)*}_{t,i}, Z^{(h)*}_{t,j}) 
    + \sum_{k=1}^{\infty}\bigl[
    \mathrm{Cov}(Z^{(h)*}_{t,i}, Z^{(h)*}_{t+k,j}) 
    + \mathrm{Cov}(Z^{(h)*}_{t+k,i}, Z^{(h)*}_{t,j})\bigr].
\]
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
Since $\boldsymbol{Z}^{(h)*}_t$ is a measurable function of the bounded 
indicator variables $(O_t, O_{t-h}, \allowbreak X_t, X_{t-h})$, it inherits strict 
stationarity and $\alpha$-mixing with exponentially decaying coefficients 
from the underlying processes by the same argument as in 
Proposition~\ref{prop:clt_zstar}, and all moment conditions are trivially satisfied. 
The result then follows from the Cramér--Wold theorem 
\citep[Corollary~4.5]{kallenberg_2021} and Ibragimov's CLT 
\citep[Theorem~7]{Ibragimov1962}.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[Asymptotic normality of $\hat{\boldsymbol{f}}^{(h)*}$]
\label{thm:clt_fhat_h}

$\hat{\boldsymbol{f}}^{(h)*}$ is consistent for $\boldsymbol{\mu}^{(h)*}$, 
and
\[
    \sqrt{n}\!\left(\hat{\boldsymbol{f}}^{(h)*} 
    - \boldsymbol{\mu}^{(h)*}\right)
    \xrightarrow{d} \mathcal{N}\!\left(\boldsymbol{0},\, 
    \boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{\boldsymbol{f}^{(h)*}}\right),
    \qquad
    \boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{\boldsymbol{f}^{(h)*}} 
    = \boldsymbol{G}\,\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{\boldsymbol{Z}^{(h)*}}\,
    \boldsymbol{G}^\top,
\]
where the Jacobian $\boldsymbol{G}_{2m\times 2(m+1)}$, evaluated at 
$\boldsymbol{\mu}_{\boldsymbol{Z}^{(h)*}}$, is given by
\begin{align*}
    \boldsymbol{G} =
    \begin{pmatrix}
    0 & \frac{-f_0}{\pi} & \frac{1}{\pi}& 0 &\cdots&&\cdots & 0 \\
    \vdots &\vdots & 0 &\ddots & \ddots &&& \vdots \\
    0 & \frac{-f_{m-1}}{\pi}& \vdots & \ddots & \frac{1}{\pi} & & &\vdots\\
    \frac{-f_{00}(h)}{\pi(h)} & 0 & & & & \frac{1}{\pi(h)}&\ddots&\vdots\\
    \vdots & \vdots & \vdots & & & \ddots & \ddots&0\\
    \frac{-f_{m-1m-1}(h)}{\pi(h)} & 0 & 0 & \cdots& 
    &\cdots & 0&\frac{1}{\pi(h)}      
    \end{pmatrix}.
\end{align*}
\end{theorem}
\subsection{Hypothesis Testing for Serial Dependence}
\label{subsec:serial-testing}
The asymptotic results derived above provide the foundation for a
formal test of serial independence based on $\hat{\kappa}_{\mathrm{ord}}(h)$.
The null and alternative hypotheses are
\[
    H_0: \kappa_{\mathrm{ord}}(h) = 0
    \qquad \text{vs.} \qquad
    H_A: \kappa_{\mathrm{ord}}(h) \neq 0.
\]
A key asymmetry between $H_0$ and $H_A$ shapes the subsequent
analysis: the asymptotic variance $\sigma^2_\kappa$ of
$\sqrt{n}\,\hat{\kappa}_{\mathrm{ord}}(h)$ is only available in
closed form under $H_0$, where the i.i.d.\ assumption on $(X_t)$
eliminates the higher-order mixed moments of $(X_t)$ that appear
in $\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{\boldsymbol{f}^{(h)*}}$ (cases
\textbf{(ix)} and \textbf{(x)} of
Section~\ref{subsec:serial-general}). Under $H_A$, the general
expression for $\sigma^2_\kappa$ is intractable without further
assumptions on the dependence structure of $(X_t)$. Consequently,
confidence intervals for the true value $\kappa_{\mathrm{ord}}(h)$
cannot be constructed under $H_A$ in the present framework.
What can be established under $H_A$ is the consistency of the test,
which is treated in Section~\ref{subsec:serial-HA}.

\subsubsection{Asymptotic null distribution, test statistic, and
confidence intervals under $H_0$}
Under the null hypothesis $H_0$ that $(X_t)$ is i.i.d., the condition 
$f_{ij}(h) = f_i f_j$ holds for all $h \geq 1$. This considerably 
simplifies the third- and fourth-order mixed moments of 
$\boldsymbol{Z}^{(h)*}_t$ appearing in cases \textbf{(ix)} and 
\textbf{(x)} of $\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{\boldsymbol{Z}^{(h)*}}$. Before 
carrying out the simplifications, we collect the relevant moments under 
$H_0$, which will be used repeatedly below:
\begin{align*}
    &\mathrm{E}[Z_{t,i}Z_{t-h,i}Z_{t-k,j}Z_{t-k-h,j}]
    - f_{ii}(h)f_{jj}(h) = \begin{cases}
        f^2_{\min\{i,j\}} - f_i^2f_j^2 & \text{if } k = 0,\\
        f_i f_j f_{\min\{i,j\}} - f_i f_j^2 & \text{if } k = h,\\
        0 & \text{else,}
    \end{cases}\\[1ex]
    &\mathrm{E}[Z_{t+k, i}Z_{t,j}Z_{t-h,j}] - f_if_{jj}(h) = \begin{cases}
        f_jf_{\min\{i,j\}} - f_i f_j^2 & \text{if } k = 0, \\
        0 & \text{else,}
    \end{cases}\\[1ex]
    &\mathrm{E}[Z_{t, i}Z_{t+k,j}Z_{t+k-h, j}] - f_i f_{jj}(h) = \begin{cases}
        f_jf_{\min\{i,j\}} - f_i f_j^2 & \text{if } k = 0 \text{ or } k = h, \\
        0 & \text{else.}
    \end{cases}
\end{align*}
Applying these simplifications to cases \textbf{(ix)} and \textbf{(x)} 
of $\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{\boldsymbol{Z}^{(h)*}}$ yields:
{\small\allowdisplaybreaks
\begin{align*}
&\textbf{(ix)}\ i = 0,\ldots,m-1,\ j = m,\ldots,2m-1 \quad(j-m = j'):\\
&\quad \overset{H_0}{=} 
\pi(h)\,f_{j}f_{\min\{i,j'\}} - \pi\pi(h)f_i f_{j'}^2 \\
&\qquad + \underbrace{\mathrm{E}[O_t^2 O_{t+h}]\,f_{j'}f_{\min\{i,j'\}} 
+ \mathrm{E}[O_{t+h} O_t O_{t-h}]\,f_i f_{j'}^2 - 2 \pi \pi(h)f_i f_{j'}^2}_{k=h} \\
&\qquad + f_i f_{j'}^2 \mathrm{E}[O_t^2 O_{t-h}] - f_i f_{j'}^2\mathrm{E}[O_t^2 O_{t-h}] \\
&\qquad + \sum_{\substack{k=1\\k\neq h}}^{\infty} \bigl[
\mathrm{E}[O_t O_{t+k} O_{t+k-h}] + \mathrm{E}[O_{t+k} O_t O_{t-h}]
- 2\pi\pi(h)\bigr] f_i f_{j'}^2 \\
&\quad =2\pi(h)f_{j'}f_{\min\{i,j'\}} - \pi \pi(h)f_i f_{j'}^2 - \pi(h)f_i f_{j'}^2
+ (1-\pi)\pi(h)f_i f_{j'}^2 - (1-\pi)\pi(h)f_i f_{j'}^2\\ 
&\qquad + f_{i}f_{j'}^2 \sum_{k=1}^{\infty} \bigl[
\mathrm{E}[O_t O_{t+k} O_{t+k-h}] + \mathrm{E}[O_{t+k} O_t O_{t-h}]
- 2\pi\pi(h)\bigr] \\
&\quad = 2\pi(h)(f_{j'}f_{\min\{i,j'\}} - f_i f_{j'}^2) + f_i f_{j'}^2 \sigma^{(h)}_{-1-2}\\[2ex]
&\textbf{(x)}\ i,j = m,\ldots,2m-1\ \quad \quad(i-m = i', j-m = j'):\\
&\quad \overset{H_0}{=} \pi(h)f_{\min\{i',j'\}}^2 - \pi(h)^2 f_{i'}^2f_{j'}^2 
+ 2 \mathrm{E}[O_t O_{t+h} O_{t-h}] f_{i'} f_{j'} f_{\min\{i',j'\}} - 2 \pi(h)^2 f_{i'}^2 f_{j'}^2 \\
&\qquad + \sum_{\substack{k=1\\k\neq h}}^{\infty} 2 f_{i'}^2 f_{j'}^2 \left[  \mathrm{E}[O_t O_{t-h} O_{t+k}O_{t+k-h}] - \pi(h)^2\right] \\
&\quad = \pi(h)f_{\min\{i', j'\}}^2 - \pi(h)^2 f_{i'}^2f_{j'}^2 
+ 2 \mathrm{E}[O_t O_{t+h} O_{t-h}] f_{i'} f_{j'} f_{\min\{i',j'\}}\\
&\qquad - 2f_{i'}^2f_{j'}^2 E[O_{t}O_{t+h}O_{t-h}] 
+ \pi(h)f_{i'}^2f_{j'}^2 - \pi(h)f_{i'}^2f_{j'}^2 + \\
&\qquad +2 f_{i'}^2f_{j'}^2 \sum_{k=1}^{\infty}  \left[  \mathrm{E}[O_t O_{t-h} O_{t+k} O_{t+k-h}] - \pi(h)^2\right] \\
&\quad = \pi(h) (f_{\min\{i',j'\}}^2- f_{i'}^2f_{j'}^2) + 2\pi(h, 2h)f_{i'}f_{j'}(f_{\min\{i',j'\}}- f_{i'}f_{j'})
+ f_{i'}^2f_{j'}^2\sigma^{(h)}_{-2-2},
\end{align*}}\noindent
where $\pi(h,2h) := \mathrm{E}[O_t O_{t+h}O_{t-h}]$.
Substituting all elements of $\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{\boldsymbol{Z}^{(h)*}}$ 
into $\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{\boldsymbol{f}^{(h)*}} = \boldsymbol{G}
\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{\boldsymbol{Z}^{(h)*}}\boldsymbol{G}^\top$ and 
carrying out the matrix multiplication under $H_0$, the elements 
$\sigma^{(h)*}_{ij}$ simplify to (see Appendix~\ref{app:derivation_covariance}):
{\allowdisplaybreaks\begin{align*}
    &\textbf{(i)}\ i,j = 0,\ldots,m-1: \\
    &\quad \sigma^{(h)*}_{ij} \overset{H_0}{=} 
        \frac{1}{\pi}\left(f_{\min\{i,j\}} - f_if_j\right) \\[1ex]
    &\textbf{(ii)}\ i = 0,\ldots,m-1,\ j = m,\ldots,2m-1\ (j'=j-m):\\
    &\quad \sigma^{(h)*}_{ij} \overset{H_0}{=} 
        \frac{2}{\pi}\,f_{j'}\!\left(f_{\min\{i,j'\}} - f_if_{j'}\right)
        = 2f_{j'}\,\sigma^{(h)*}_{ij'} \\[1ex]
    &\textbf{(iii)}\ i,j = m,\ldots,2m-1\ (i'=i-m,\ j'=j-m):\\
    &\quad \sigma^{(h)*}_{ij} \overset{H_0}{=} 
        \frac{1}{\pi(h)^2}(f_{\min\{i',j'\}}-f_{i'}f_{j'})
        \Bigl(\pi(h)(f_{\min\{i',j'\}}-f_{i'}f_{j'}) 
        + 2\pi(h,2h)f_{i'}f_{j'}\Bigr)
\end{align*}}\noindent
Applying the delta method to 
$\kappa_{\mathrm{ord}} = A/B$, where 
$A = \sum_{j=0}^{m-1}(f_{jj}(h)-f_j^2)$ and 
$B = \sum_{j=0}^{m-1}f_j(1-f_j)$, the relevant Jacobian is
\[
    \boldsymbol{D}_{\kappa} := 
    \left(d_{10},\ldots,d_{1m-1},\,d_{20},\ldots,d_{2m-1}\right),
\]
where for $i = 0,\ldots,m-1$:
\begin{align*}
    \textbf{(i)}\ d_{1;i} 
    &= \frac{\partial\kappa_{\mathrm{ord}}}{\partial f_i}
    = \frac{-2f_i}{B} - \frac{(1-2f_i)A}{B^2}, \\
    \textbf{(ii)}\ d_{2;i} 
    &= \frac{\partial\kappa_{\mathrm{ord}}}{\partial f_{ii}(h)}
    = \frac{1}{B}.
\end{align*}
Plugging the derivatives and the elements of 
$\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{\boldsymbol{f}^{(h)*}}$ into the delta method 
formula and evaluating under $H_0$ yields (see Appendix~\ref{app:var-kappa} 
for the detailed calculation)
\begin{align*}
    \sigma^2_\kappa 
    = \frac{\frac{1}{\pi(h)}\displaystyle\sum_{i,j=0}^{m-1} 
      \bigl(f_{\min\{i,j\}}-f_if_j\bigr)
      \Bigl(f_{\min\{i,j\}} 
      + \Bigl(1 + \dfrac{2\pi(h,2h)}{\pi(h)} 
      - \dfrac{4\pi(h)}{\pi}\Bigr)f_if_j\Bigr)}
     {\left(\displaystyle\sum_{k=0}^{m-1}f_k(1-f_k)\right)^2}.
\end{align*}
Building on this, we now investigate the finite-sample bias by means 
of a second-order Taylor expansion. Since the linear term 
$\boldsymbol{D}_\kappa(\hat{\boldsymbol{f}}^{(h)*} - 
\boldsymbol{\mu}^{(h)*})$ has expectation of order $o(n^{-1})$, 
it does not contribute to the leading bias term, and the $O(n^{-1})$ 
bias reduces by Lemma~\ref{lem:quadform} to
\[
    \mathrm{Bias}(n\hat{\kappa}_{\mathrm{ord}})
    = \frac{1}{2}\,\mathrm{tr}\!\left(
      \boldsymbol{H}_{\kappa}\,\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{\boldsymbol{f}^{(h)*}}
      \right) + o(n^{-1}).
\]
The elements of the Hessian $\boldsymbol{H}_{\kappa}$ are given by
\begin{align*}
    \frac{\partial d_{1i}}{\partial f_j} 
    &= \begin{cases}
        2\!\left(\dfrac{2f_i-4f_i^2}{B^2} - \dfrac{1}{B}\right) 
        & \text{if } i = j, \\[1ex]
        2\,\dfrac{f_i+f_j-4f_if_j}{B^2} & \text{if } i\neq j,
    \end{cases} \\[1ex]
    \frac{\partial d_{1i}}{\partial f_{jj}(h)} 
    &= \frac{-(1-2f_i)}{B^2}, \qquad
    \frac{\partial d_{2i}}{\partial f_j} 
    = \frac{-(1-2f_j)}{B^2}, \qquad
    \frac{\partial d_{2i}}{\partial f_{jj}(h)} = 0,
\end{align*}
with detailed derivations given in Appendix~\ref{app:Hessian}.
Evaluating $\frac{1}{2}\mathrm{tr}(\boldsymbol{H}_\kappa 
\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{\boldsymbol{f}^{(h)*}})$ under $H_0$, using 
$\sigma^{(h)*}_{im+l} = 2f_l\sigma^{(h)*}_{il}$ from case 
\textbf{(ii)} of $\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{\boldsymbol{f}^{(h)*}}$ and 
symmetry $\sigma^{(h)*}_{il} = \sigma^{(h)*}_{li}$, yields
\[
    \mathrm{Bias}(\hat{\kappa}_{\mathrm{ord}}(h)) 
    \overset{H_0}{=} -\frac{1}{n\pi}.
\]
The detailed calculation is carried out in Appendix~\ref{app:Bias}.
The results, that correspond to Theorem~3 in \citet{weiss2021categorical}, are summarized in the following theorem.

\begin{theorem}[Asymptotic distribution and bias of 
$\hat{\kappa}_{\mathrm{ord}}(h)$]
\label{thm:kappa-asymp}
Let the assumptions of Theorem~\ref{thm:clt_fstarhat} hold and denote
\[
    \pi(h_1,\ldots,h_r) := \mathrm{E}[O_0\cdot O_{h_1}\cdots O_{h_r}].
\]
Then, under the null hypothesis $H_0$ that $(X_t)$ is i.i.d., the 
following hold as $n\to\infty$:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Asymptotic normality.} The estimator 
    $\hat{\kappa}_{\mathrm{ord}}(h)$ satisfies
    \[
        \sqrt{n}\,\hat{\kappa}_{\mathrm{ord}}(h) 
        \xrightarrow{d} \mathcal{N}(0,\sigma^2_\kappa),
    \]
    where
    \[
        \sigma^2_\kappa =
        \frac{
            \frac{1}{\pi(h)}\displaystyle\sum_{i,j=0}^{m-1}
            \bigl(f_{\min\{i,j\}}-f_if_j\bigr)
            \Bigl(f_{\min\{i,j\}} 
            + \Bigl(1+\frac{2\pi(h,2h)}{\pi(h)} 
            - \frac{4\pi(h)}{\pi}\Bigr)f_if_j\Bigr)
        }{
            \left(\displaystyle\sum_{k=0}^{m-1}f_k(1-f_k)\right)^2
        }.
    \]
    \item \textbf{Finite-sample bias.} The bias of 
    $\hat{\kappa}_{\mathrm{ord}}(h)$ under $H_0$ is
    \[
        \mathrm{Bias}\bigl(\hat{\kappa}_{\mathrm{ord}}(h)\bigr) 
        = -\frac{1}{n \pi}+ o(n^{-1}),
    \]
    so that the bias-corrected estimator is
    \[
        \hat{\kappa}^{\mathrm{bc}}_{\mathrm{ord}}(h) 
        := \hat{\kappa}_{\mathrm{ord}}(h) + \frac{1}{n\pi}.
    \]
\end{enumerate}
\end{theorem}

\begin{remark}[Simplification under i.i.d. observation process]
\label{rem:iid_Ot}
Suppose in addition that the observation process $(O_t)$ is
i.i.d. Then the joint moments factorize as
\[
    \pi(h) = \mathrm{E}[O_t O_{t-h}] = \pi^2,
    \qquad
    \pi(h,2h) = \mathrm{E}[O_t O_{t-h} O_{t-2h}] = \pi^3.
\]
Substituting into $\sigma^2_\kappa$ yields (see Appendix~\ref{app:var-kappa-iid} for the detailed calculation)
\[
    \sigma^2_\kappa =
    \frac{1}{\pi^2}\frac{
        \displaystyle\sum_{i,j=0}^{m-1}
        \bigl(f_{\min\{i,j\}}-f_if_j\bigr)
        \Bigl(f_{\min\{i,j\}} 
        + (1-2\pi)f_if_j\Bigr)
    }{
        \left(\displaystyle\sum_{k=0}^{m-1}f_k(1-f_k)\right)^2
    }.
\]
\end{remark}
\paragraph{Test statistic and confidence intervals.}
Under $H_0$, Theorem~\ref{thm:kappa-asymp} and Slutsky's theorem  \citep[Theorem~13.18]{klenke2005}
immediately yield a consistent plug-in test. Replacing all unknown
quantities in $\sigma^2_\kappa$ by their sample counterparts,
specifically $f_i$ by $\hat{f}^*_i$, the bivariate probabilities
$f_{ij}(h)$ by $\hat{f}^*_{ij}(h)$, and the observation-process
moments $\pi$, $\pi(h)$, and $\pi(h,2h)$ by
\begin{align*}
    \hat{\pi}      &:= \bar{O}
        = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{t=1}^n O_t, \quad
    \widehat{\pi(h)}   := \overline{OO}_{\,h}
        = \frac{1}{n-h}\sum_{t=h+1}^n O_t O_{t-h}, \\
    \widehat{\pi(h,2h)} &:= \frac{1}{n-2h}
        \sum_{t=2h+1}^n O_t O_{t+h} O_{t-h},
\end{align*}
yields a consistent estimator $\hat{\sigma}^2_\kappa$ of
$\sigma^2_\kappa$. Consistency follows from the continuous mapping
theorem together with the consistency of $\hat{f}^*_i$,
$\hat{f}^*_{ij}(h)$ (Theorems~\ref{thm:clt_fstarhat}
and~\ref{thm:clt_fhat_h}) and the law of large numbers applied to
the observation-process moments.\\
Alternatively, one may test for serial independence of the observation 
process $(O_t)$ using, for example, a portmanteau test such as the Ljung–Box 
test (see \cite[Chapter~1.6]{brockwell_davis_2016}). Note that the Ljung-Box test
only tests for linear dependence structures and may fail to detect nonlinear dependence in $(O_t)$.
Since higher moments of $O_t$ are directly determined by its first moment (since $O_t \in \{0,1\}$),
the implied limitations of the Ljung-Box test for binary time series are less severe than for continuous-valued 
time series. However, since there also may be dependence involving products like $O_t O_{t-h} O_{t+k}$
that is not captured by the Ljung-Box test, the assumption of i.i.d. $O_t$ should be adopted with care.
If the null hypothesis of no serial dependence cannot
be rejected, it is justified to assume that $(O_t)$ is i.i.d., in which
case the simplified variance expression in
Remark~\ref{rem:iid_Ot} can be used. In this situation, only $\pi$
needs to be estimated, which can improve numerical stability of the
plug-in estimator. If, however, serial dependence is detected, the
general estimator based on $\hat{\pi}(h)$ and
$\hat{\pi}(h,2h)$ should be preferred.\\
The test statistic as proposed by \citet{weiss2021categorical} is
\[
    T_n(h) := \frac{\sqrt{n}\,\hat{\kappa}_{\mathrm{ord}}(h)}
                   {\hat{\sigma}_\kappa},
\]
and a test of level $\alpha$ rejects $H_0$ whenever
$|T_n(h)| > z_{\alpha/2}$, where $z_{\alpha/2}$ denotes the
$(1-\alpha/2)$-quantile of the standard normal distribution.
Since $T_n(h) \xrightarrow{d} \mathcal{N}(0,1)$ under $H_0$ by
Theorem~\ref{thm:kappa-asymp} and Slutsky's theorem, this test has
asymptotic level $\alpha$.\\
The test can equivalently be expressed through a confidence interval:
$H_0$ is rejected at level $\alpha$ if and only if $0$ lies outside
\begin{equation*}\label{eq:ci-kappa}
    \hat{\kappa}_{\mathrm{ord}}(h)
    \;\pm\;
    z_{\alpha/2}\,\frac{\hat{\sigma}_\kappa}{\sqrt{n}}.
\end{equation*}
It is important to note that \eqref{eq:ci-kappa} is an interval for
the purpose of testing $H_0: \kappa_{\mathrm{ord}}(h) = 0$; it does
\emph{not} serve as a confidence interval for the true value
$\kappa_{\mathrm{ord}}(h)$ under $H_A$, as the asymptotic variance
of $\hat{\kappa}_{\mathrm{ord}}(h)$ differs from $\sigma^2_\kappa$
when $(X_t)$ is not i.i.d.\\
Also note that $\hat{\kappa}_{\mathrm{ord}}(h)$ for a fixed lag $h$ 
constitutes a necessary but not sufficient condition for the i.i.d.\ 
null hypothesis: rejection at any lag $h$ implies departure from 
i.i.d., but non-rejection at a single lag does not rule out 
dependence at other lags or through off-diagonal bivariate 
probabilities. In practice, the test is therefore applied 
simultaneously for several lags $h = 1, \ldots, H$.\\
When testing simultaneously for $h = 1, \ldots, H$, a multiple testing 
problem arises: even under the i.i.d.\ null hypothesis, the probability 
of falsely rejecting at least one individual test exceeds the nominal 
level $\alpha$. This quantity, known as the \emph{familywise error rate}, 
grows with $H$. There are various more or less conservative methods to 
control it, such as the Bonferroni correction, which rejects if 
$|T_n(h)| > z_{\alpha/(2H)}$ for any $h$, or the less conservative 
Holm procedure, which orders the $H$ $p$-values as 
$p_{(1)} \leq \ldots \leq p_{(H)}$ and rejects the hypothesis 
associated with $p_{(k)}$ if $p_{(l)} \leq \alpha/(H+1-l)$ for all 
$l \leq k$. For a comprehensive treatment of these and further 
procedures, see \citet[pp.~348--351]{lehmann_romano_2005}.

\subsection{Consistency under $H_A$}
\label{subsec:serial-HA}

Under the alternative hypothesis $H_A$, the process $(X_t)$ is not i.i.d.,
so that there exists at least one lag $h \ge 1$ with 
$\kappa_{\mathrm{ord}}(h) \neq 0$.\\
The estimator $\hat{\kappa}_{\mathrm{ord}}(h)$ is a continuous function 
of the empirical frequencies $\hat{f}^*_i$ and $\hat{f}^*_{ii}(h)$.
Since these are consistent estimators of $f_i$ and $f_{ii}(h)$ (see Theorem~\ref{thm:clt_fstarhat} and Theorem~\ref{thm:clt_fhat_h}), 
the continuous mapping theorem (see, e.g., \citealp[Theorem 3.27]{kallenberg_2021}) implies
\[
\hat{\kappa}_{\mathrm{ord}}(h) \xrightarrow{p} 
\kappa_{\mathrm{ord}}(h) \neq 0.
\]
It follows that
\[
\sqrt{n}\,\hat{\kappa}_{\mathrm{ord}}(h) \xrightarrow{p} \infty,
\]
since $\hat{\kappa}_{\mathrm{ord}}(h)$ converges to a nonzero constant.\\
Therefore, the test statistic diverges under $H_A$, implying that 
the rejection probability converges to one. This establishes the 
consistency of the test.

\chapter{Simulation Study}
\label{chap:simulation}

The theoretical results derived in the preceding chapters characterize 
the asymptotic behavior of the proposed estimators, but leave open the 
question of how well these approximations perform in finite samples. 
In particular, the bias corrections derived for the IOV estimator 
(Corollary~\ref{cor:iov_missing}) and for $\hat{\kappa}_{\mathrm{ord}}(h)$ (Theorem~\ref{thm:kappa-asymp}) 
are asymptotic results, and it is not a priori clear at what sample 
sizes they provide reliable guidance. This chapter therefore complements 
the theoretical analysis with a Monte Carlo simulation study, pursuing 
three goals: first, to assess the finite-sample accuracy of the 
asymptotic bias formulas; second, to investigate the rate of convergence 
of the estimators to their asymptotic normal distributions; and third, 
to examine the sensitivity of the estimators to violations of the MCAR 
assumption.

\section{Setup and Design}
\label{sec:sim-setup}

The setup of this simulation study extends the illustrative simulations in \citet{weiss2021categorical} by considering a substantially 
broader range of sample sizes, both scenarios simultaneously, and additional missingness mechanisms beyond i.i.d.\ Bernoulli observations.
\subsection*{Data generating process}

All simulations are based on the BinAR(1) process as described in 
\citet[Section~3.3]{Weiss2018}, which serves as a flexible and tractable 
model for ordinal categorical time series with controllable serial dependence. Note that 

\paragraph{Model definition.}
The BinAR(1) process is parameterized by the number of categories 
$m \in \mathbb{N}$, the marginal probability $p \in (0,1)$, and the 
thinning parameter 
\[
r \in \left(\max\left\{-\frac{p}{1-p}, -\frac{1-p}{p}\right\}, 1\right).
\]
It is defined recursively by
\[
X_t = B(X_{t-1},\, \alpha) + B(m - X_{t-1},\, \beta),
\]
where $B(k, q)$ denotes a binomial random variable with parameters 
$k$ and $q$, and where $\alpha = p(1-r) + r$ and $\beta = p(1-r)$. 
This representation has a simple interpretation: units currently in state 
“1” persist with probability $\alpha$, while units in state “0” switch to 
state “1” with probability $\beta$. The parameter $r$ controls the strength and direction of serial 
dependence: $r = 0$ yields an i.i.d.\ process, positive values imply 
positive autocorrelation, and negative values imply negative autocorrelation.

\paragraph{Distributional properties.}
The stationary marginal distribution of $(X_t)$ is \linebreak
$\mathrm{Binomial}(m, p)$,  so that the process is strictly stationary. 
In particular, the true CDF vector $\boldsymbol{f}$ and the index of 
ordinal variation (IOV) are available in closed form as
\[
f_i = P(X_t \leq i) = \sum_{k=0}^{i}\binom{m}{k}p^k(1-p)^{m-k}, 
\qquad i = 0,\ldots,m-1,
\]
\[
\mathrm{IOV} = \frac{4}{m}\sum_{i=0}^{m-1} f_i(1-f_i).
\]
The one-step-ahead transition probabilities
\[
p_{i|j}(1) := P(X_t = i \mid X_{t-1} = j)
\]
are given by \citep[p.~60]{Weiss2018}:
\[
p_{i|j}(1)= \sum^{\min\{i,j\}}_{q = \max\{0, i+j-m \}}
\binom{j}{q} \binom{m-j}{i-q}\alpha^q (1-\alpha)^{j-q} 
\beta^{i-q} (1-\beta)^{m-j+q-i}.
\]
The $h$-step-ahead transition probabilities are obtained by replacing 
$\alpha$ and $\beta$ with $\alpha_h = p(1-r^h) + r^h$ and 
$\beta_h = p(1-r^h)$. The lag-$h$ joint probabilities
\[
p_{ij}(h) = P(X_t = i, X_{t-h} = j)
\]
are then given by
\[
p_{ij}(h) = p_{i|j}(h)\cdot p_j,
\]
where $p_j = P(X_t = j)$. The corresponding joint CDF components are
\[
f_{ij}(h) = \sum_{k=0}^i \sum_{l=0}^j p_{kl}(h).
\]
Hence, $\kappa_{\mathrm{ord}}(h)$ is available in closed form by 
plugging the relevant $f_{ij}(h)$ and $f_i$ into 
Definition~\ref{def:cohens-kappa}. Figure~\ref{fig:kappa-profile} 
illustrates the serial dependence structure of the BinAR(1) process 
by displaying the analytically computed values of 
$\kappa_{\mathrm{ord}}(h)$ as a function of the lag $h$ for 
Scenario B with $r \in \{-0.5, -0.2,0.2, 0.4,  0.6, 0.8, 0.95  \}$. 
For $r > 0$, the curves decay exponentially with $h$, 
with the rate of decay decreasing for higher values of $r$ — 
reflecting the stronger persistence of the process. For $r < 0$, 
$\kappa_{\mathrm{ord}}(h)$ alternates in sign: negative at odd lags 
and positive at even lags, while its absolute value decays 
exponentially to zero as $h \to \infty$.
\begin{figure}[htbp]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\textwidth, height = 6cm]{../Graphs/Cohens_Kappa_by_h.png}
    \caption{Cohen's $\kappa_{\mathrm{ord}}(h)$ for different values of $r$.}
    \label{fig:kappa-profile}
\end{figure}
\paragraph{Mixing properties.}
Finally, we verify that the BinAR(1) process satisfies the dependence 
assumptions required for the asymptotic theory. Since the BinAR(1) process 
is an ergodic Markov chain on the finite state space $\{0,\ldots,m\}$ 
\citep[p.~60]{Weiss2018}, it follows that the process is $\phi$-mixing 
with exponentially decaying coefficients, i.e.\ there exist constants 
$C > 0$ and $\rho \in (0,1)$ such that $\phi(n) \le C \rho^n$ for all $n$ 
\citep[Appendix~B.2.2]{Weiss2018}. \\
Moreover, the mixing coefficients satisfy the inequality 
$2\alpha(n) \le \phi(n)$ for all $n$ \citep[Eq.~(1.11)]{bradley_2005}. In particular, this also implies
\[\alpha(n) \leq \phi(n) \leq C \rho^n ~ \text{for some} ~ C > 0, \rho \in (0,1),\]
showing that the BinAR(1) process satisfies the mixing-assumptions of Theorem~\ref{thm:clt_fstarhat}.
\paragraph{Missingness mechanism.}
Missingness is introduced via the amplitude modulation framework of 
Section~\ref{sec:Amp Mod}. We consider three different data-generating mechanisms 
for the observation process $(O_t)$.\\
First, as a baseline, we assume independent missingness, where 
$(O_t)$ is i.i.d.\ Bernoulli distributed with constant observation probability 
$\pi \in (0,1]$, i.e.
\[
O_t \sim \mathrm{Bernoulli}(\pi).
\]
Second, we consider a serially dependent observation process, where 
$(O_t)$ is a stationary binary Markov chain on $\{0,1\}$ with marginal 
observation probability $\mathrm{E}[O_t] = \pi$ and autocorrelation 
parameter $r_{\pi} \in (0,1)$, with transition probabilities
\[
    P(O_t = 1 \mid O_{t-1} = 1) = (1-r_{\pi})\pi+ r_{\pi}, \qquad
    P(O_t = 1 \mid O_{t-1} = 0) = \pi(1-r_{\pi}).
\]
This mechanism preserves the marginal observation probability $\pi$ 
while inducing serial dependence in $(O_t)$, with 
$\mathrm{Cov}(O_t, O_{t+h}) = r_{\pi}^h\,\pi(1-\pi)$ for all $h \geq 1$ 
\citep[p.~139]{Weiss2018}. Notably, this process is equal to a BinAR(1)-process 
with parameters $m =1,~p = \pi$ and $r = r_h$, which implies that the process 
satisfies the relevant mixing conditions.\\
Third, we consider observation schemes that depend on the current state of 
the underlying process $(X_t)$, corresponding to MNAR-type mechanisms. In 
the first variant, the observation probability increases linearly with $X_t$, i.e.
\[
\pi_t = \pi_{\text{low}} + (\pi_{\text{high}} - \pi_{\text{low}})\frac{X_t}{m},
\]
while in the second variant it decreases linearly with $X_t$, i.e.
\[
\pi_t = \pi_{\text{high}} - (\pi_{\text{high}} - \pi_{\text{low}})\frac{X_t}{m}.
\]
To be able to reference both mechanisms in a more concise way, we will write \linebreak $P(O_t= 1)\propto X_t$ 
and $P(O_t=1)\propto-X_t$ to refer to the increasing and decreasing versions, respectively. 
The parameters $\pi_{\text{low}}$ and $\pi_{\text{high}}$ define the minimum and maximum observation probabilities, 
respectively, and therefore determine the range in which $\pi_t$ can vary. In both cases,
\[
O_t \mid X_t \sim \mathrm{Bernoulli}(\pi_t).
\]
These three mechanisms allow us to study independent, serially dependent, 
and state-dependent missingness within a unified simulation framework.

\subsection*{Simulation scenarios}

Two combinations of $(m, p, r)$ are considered, chosen to represent 
qualitatively different settings:
\begin{itemize}

    $m = 3$, $p = 0.20$, $r = 0.35$ — 
    a coarse ordinal scale with few categories, a skewed marginal 
    distribution, and moderate serial dependence.

    \item \textbf{Scenario B:}\phantomsection\label{scen:B}
    $m = 10$, $p = 0.45$, $r = 0.50$ — 
    a finer ordinal scale, a near-symmetric marginal distribution, 
    and stronger serial dependence.
\end{itemize}

The sample size $n$ and the number of Monte Carlo replications $B$ vary

\subsection*{Estimators and performance measures}
Depending on the scenario under consideration, a subset of the 
following quantities is computed from the observed data 
$(O_t X_t)_{t=1}^n$: the corrected CDF estimator 
$\hat{\boldsymbol{f}}^*$ (Definition~\ref{def:correctedest}), the 
IOV estimator $\widehat{\mathrm{IOV}}$, the skewness estimator 
$\widehat{\mathrm{skew}}$, and the ordinal Cohen's $\kappa$ estimator 
$\hat{\kappa}_{\mathrm{ord}}(h)$ for lags $h \in \{1, 2\}$. The 
specific estimator(s) reported are stated explicitly for each 
figure or table.\\
Performance is generally assessed through a subset of the following 
measures, averaged across replications:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Mean bias:} $\frac{1}{B}\sum_{b=1}^B 
    (\hat{\theta}_b - \theta)$, where $\theta$ denotes the true 
    parameter value.
    \item \textbf{$n$-scaled bias:} $n \cdot \frac{1}{B}\sum_{b=1}^B 
    (\hat{\theta}_b - \theta)$, used to assess convergence to the 
    theoretical $O(n^{-1})$ bias limit.
    \item \textbf{Standard deviation} across replications, compared 
    against the asymptotic standard deviation 
    $\sqrt{\sigma^2_\theta / n}$ derived in the theoretical chapters.
\end{itemize}
The theoretical bias and asymptotic variance expressions for  $\widehat{\mathrm{IOV}}$ and $\hat{\kappa}(h)$ used as 
benchmarks throughout this section come from Corollary~\ref{cor:iov_missing} and Theorem~\ref{thm:kappa-asymp} respectively.

\chapter{Extension to Nominal Processes}
\label{chap:nominal}

The theoretical framework developed in the preceding chapters was 
presented for ordinal categorical processes, where the natural ordering 
of the categories allows the marginal distribution to be represented 
through the CDF vector $\boldsymbol{f}$ and its sample analogue 
$\hat{\boldsymbol{f}}$, as also considered by \citet{weiss2021categorical}. 
For nominal processes, no such ordering exists, 
and the marginal distribution is instead characterized by the PMF vector 
$\boldsymbol{p}$ and its estimator $\hat{\boldsymbol{p}}$ (recall 
Definition~\ref{def:empirical_estimators}). Since both estimators are sample 
means of bounded indicator processes, and since we again assume $(X_t)$ to be strictly stationary and
$\alpha$-mixing with exponentially decaying coefficients
(Definition~\ref{def:mixing}), the asymptotic theory carries over almost entirely. 
This chapter makes this correspondence precise and focuses on the points 
where the nominal case genuinely differs: the choice of dispersion 
measure and the form of the serial dependence measure.

\section{Asymptotic Distribution of $\hat{\boldsymbol{p}}$}

The structure of $\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_Y$
differs from that of $\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_Z$, however, in the lag-0
term. For ordinal data, the joint expectation
$\mathrm{E}[Z_{t,i}Z_{t,j}] = P(X_t \leq s_i, X_t \leq s_j) =
f_{\min\{i,j\}}$ exploits the cumulative structure of the
binarization. For nominal data, no such ordering exists, and instead
$\mathrm{E}[Y_{t,i}Y_{t,j}] = P(X_t = s_i, X_t = s_j)$, which equals
$p_i$ if $i = j$ and zero otherwise. Using the Kronecker delta
$\delta_{ij} := \mathbb{I}\{i = j\}$, this can be written compactly
as $\mathrm{E}[Y_{t,i}Y_{t,j}] = \delta_{ij}p_i$, so that
$\mathrm{Cov}(Y_{t,i}, Y_{t,j}) = p_j(\delta_{ij} - p_i)$,
yielding the following result.

\begin{theorem}[Asymptotic Normality of $\hat{\boldsymbol{p}}$]
\label{thm:clt_phat}
Let $(X_t)$ be strictly stationary and $\alpha$-mixing with
exponentially decaying coefficients. Then
\[
    \sqrt{n}\bigl(\hat{\boldsymbol{p}} - \boldsymbol{p}\bigr)
    \xrightarrow[n\to\infty]{d}
    \mathcal{N}\bigl(\boldsymbol{0},\, \boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{\boldsymbol{Y}}\bigr),
\]
where $\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{\boldsymbol{Y}} = (\sigma_{\boldsymbol{Y};ij})_{i,j=0,\ldots,m}$ is
given by
\[
    \sigma_{\boldsymbol{Y};ij}
    = p_j(\delta_{ij} - p_i)
    + \sum_{h=1}^{\infty}\bigl(p_{ij}(h) + p_{ji}(h) - 2p_ip_j\bigr).
\]
\end{theorem}
\section{Asymptotic Properties of the IQV}

Since $\mathrm{IQV}(\boldsymbol{p}) $ is a quadratic polynomial in $\boldsymbol{p}$, all
third- and higher-order partial derivatives vanish. Consequently, the
second-order Taylor expansion is exact. The Jacobian and Hessian are
\[
    \boldsymbol{j}_{\mathrm{IQV}}
    = -\frac{2(m+1)}{m}(p_0, \ldots, p_m),
    \qquad
    \boldsymbol{H}_{\mathrm{IQV}}
    = -\frac{2(m+1)}{m}\boldsymbol{I}_{m+1},
\]
and the following result is obtained by the same argument as
Theorem~\ref{thm:iov_asymp}, with $\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_Z$ replaced
by $\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_Y$ throughout:

\begin{proposition}[Asymptotic Distribution of $\widehat{\mathrm{IQV}}$]
\label{prop:iqv_asymp}
Under the assumptions of Theorem~\ref{thm:clt_phat}:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item[\normalfont(i)] \textbf{Asymptotic variance:}
    \[
        \sqrt{n}\,\mathrm{Var}\bigl(\widehat{\mathrm{IQV}}\bigr)
        \overset{\mathrm{asy.}}{=}
        \frac{4(m+1)^2}{m^2}
        \sum_{i,j=0}^{m} p_i p_j\,\sigma_{Y;ij}.
    \]
    \item[\normalfont(ii)] \textbf{Bias to order $n^{-1}$:}
    \[
        \mathrm{Bias}\bigl(\widehat{\mathrm{IQV}}\bigr)
        = -\frac{2(m+1)}{mn}\sum_{i=0}^{m}\sigma_{Y;ii}.
    \]
\end{enumerate}
\end{proposition}

\section{Missingness}
\paragraph{Marginal measures}
The amplitude modulation framework of Section~\ref{sec:Amp Mod} extends 
to nominal processes without modification: the observation indicator 
$O_t$ multiplies the binarization vector $\boldsymbol{Y}_t$ rather than 
$\boldsymbol{Z}_t$, and the corrected estimator of $\boldsymbol{p}$ is 
defined analogously to Definition~\ref{def:correctedest} as

\begin{definition}[Corrected estimator $\hat{\boldsymbol{p}}^*$]\label{def:p_starhat}
\[
    \hat{\boldsymbol{p}}^*
    := \frac{\frac{1}{n}\sum_{t=1}^n O_t \boldsymbol{Y}_t}
            {\frac{1}{n}\sum_{t=1}^n O_t}
    = \frac{\hat{\boldsymbol{\mathfrak{p}}}}{\bar{O}}.
\]
\end{definition}\noindent
Since $O_t\boldsymbol{Y}_t$ is a bounded indicator process that 
inherits stationarity and $\alpha$-mixing from $(O_t)$ and $(X_t)$ 
by the same argument as in Section~\ref{sec:Amp Mod}, the asymptotic 
normality of $\hat{\boldsymbol{p}}^*$ follows from 
Theorem~\ref{thm:clt_fstarhat} with $\boldsymbol{Z}_t^*$ replaced by 
$\boldsymbol{Y}_t^* := O_t(1, \boldsymbol{Y}_t^\top)^\top$. The 
asymptotic covariance matrix $\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{\hat{\boldsymbol{p}}^*}$ 
takes the same form as \hyperref[thm:clt_fstarhat]{$\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{\hat{\boldsymbol{f}}^*}$} with $f_i$, $f_{ij}(h)$ 
replaced by $p_i$, $p_{ij}(h)$ and $(f_{\mathrm{min}\{i,j\}}- f_i f_j)$ by $p_j(\delta_{ij} - p_i)$ throughout. 
The results are summarized in the following theorem: 
\begin{theorem}[Asymptotic Normality of $\hat{\boldsymbol{p}}^*$]
\label{thm:clt_pstarhat}
Let the same assumptions regarding $X_t$ and $O_t$ hold as in Theorem~\ref{thm:clt_fstarhat}, except that $X_t$ is nominal instead of ordinal. Then
the corrected estimator $\hat{\boldsymbol{p}}^*$ (Definition \ref{def:p_starhat}) satisifies the following multivariate CLT: 
\[
    \sqrt{n}\bigl(\hat{\boldsymbol{p}}^* - \boldsymbol{p}\bigr)
    \xrightarrow[n\to\infty]{d}
    \mathcal{N}\bigl(\boldsymbol{0},\, \boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{\boldsymbol{p}^*}\bigr),
\]
where $\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{\boldsymbol{p}^*} = (\sigma_{\boldsymbol{p}^*;i,j})_{i,j=0,\ldots,m}$ is
given by
\[
    \sigma_{\boldsymbol{p}^*;i,j}
    =  \frac{1}{\pi}p_j(\delta_{ij} - p_i)
    + \frac{1}{\pi^2}\sum_{h=1}^{\infty}\bigl(p_{ij}(h) + p_{ji}(h) - 2p_i p_j\bigr).
\]
\end{theorem}
\noindent As for
\hyperref[thm:clt_fstarhat]{$\hat{\boldsymbol{f}}^*$},
the second-order term in the Taylor expansion vanishes identically, 
implying that no second-order asymptotic bias occurs. The detailed calculations 
for the asymptotic variance and bias are provided in Appendix~\ref{app:pstar}.
\paragraph{Serial dependence}
For nominal data, the serial dependence measure $\kappa_{\mathrm{nom}}$ depends on the bivariate 
probabilities $p_{ij}(h)$. We therefore define the bias-corrected estimator
\[
\hat{p}_{ij}^*(h)
=
\frac{\sum_{t=h+1}^{n}O_t O_{t-h}Y_{t,i}Y_{t-h,j}}
     {\sum_{t=h+1}^{n}O_t O_{t-h}},
\]
which is the direct analogue of $\hat f_{ij}^*(h)$ from Section~\ref{sec:serial-dependence}. Using the same arguments as for ordinal data,
we define 
\[\hat{\boldsymbol{p}}^{(h)*} := (\hat{p}^*_0, \ldots, \hat{p}^*_m, \hat{p}^*_{00}(h),  \ldots, \hat{p}^{*}_{mm}(h))^\top.\]
From here, one obtains the following multivariate central limit theorem (see Appendix~\ref{app:nominal-serial} for the detailed derivation):
\vspace{0.5cm}
\begin{proposition}[Asymptotic normality of $\hat{\boldsymbol{p}}^{(h)*}$]\label{prop:clt_phat_h}
Let the assumptions of Theorem~\ref{thm:clt_fstarhat} hold, except that $X_t$ is nominal instead of ordinal. Then 
$\hat{\boldsymbol{p}}^{(h)*}$ is consistent for $\boldsymbol{\mu}^{(h)*}$, 
and
\[
    \sqrt{n}\!\left(\hat{\boldsymbol{p}}^{(h)*} 
    - \boldsymbol{\mu}^{(h)*}\right)
    \xrightarrow{d} \mathcal{N}\!\left(\boldsymbol{0},\, 
    \boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{\boldsymbol{p}^{(h)*}}\right),
    \qquad
    \boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{\boldsymbol{p}^{(h)*}} 
    = \boldsymbol{G}\,\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{\boldsymbol{Y}^{(h)*}}\,
    \boldsymbol{G}^\top,
\]
\end{proposition}\vspace{1cm}
\noindent Under $H_0$, where $(X_t)$ is i.i.d., the joint probabilities factorize as
\[
p_{ij}(h)=p_ip_j,
\]
which leads to substantial simplifications of the asymptotic covariance matrix. 
Applying the delta method to Cohen's kappa then yields a closed-form expression for 
the asymptotic results of $\hat{\kappa}_{\mathrm{nom}}(h)$:

\begin{theorem}[Asymptotic distribution and bias of 
$\hat{\kappa}_{\mathrm{nom}}(h)$]
\label{prop:kappanom-asymp}
Let the assumptions of Theorem~\ref{prop:clt_phat_h} hold and denote
\[
    \pi(h_1,\ldots,h_r) := \mathrm{E}[O_0\cdot O_{h_1}\cdots O_{h_r}].
\]
Then, under the null hypothesis $H_0$ that $(X_t)$ is i.i.d., the 
following hold as $n\to\infty$:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Asymptotic normality.} The estimator 
    $\hat{\kappa}_{\mathrm{nom}}(h)$ satisfies
    \[
        \sqrt{n}\,\hat{\kappa}_{\mathrm{nom}}(h) 
        \xrightarrow{d} \mathcal{N}(0,\sigma^2_\kappa),
    \]
    where
    \[
        \sigma^2_\kappa =
        \frac{1}{(1-\sum_{i=1}^{m}p_i^2)^2} \frac{1}{\pi(h)} \sum_{i, j = 0}^{m}p_j^2 
        (\delta_{ij}-p_i)\bigl(\delta_{ij} +  p_i (1+ 2\frac{\pi(h, 2h)}{\pi(h)} -
         4\frac{\pi(h)}{\pi})\bigr).
    \]
    \item \textbf{Finite-sample bias.} The bias of 
    $\hat{\kappa}_{\mathrm{nom}}(h)$ under $H_0$ is
    \[
        \mathrm{Bias}\bigl(\hat{\kappa}_{\mathrm{nom}}(h)\bigr) 
        = -\frac{1}{n \pi}+ o(n^{-1}),
    \]
    so that the bias-corrected estimator is
    \[
        \hat{\kappa}^{\mathrm{bc}}_{\mathrm{nom}}(h) 
        := \hat{\kappa}_{\mathrm{nom}}(h) + \frac{1}{n\pi}.
    \]
\end{enumerate}
\end{theorem}
\noindent The derivation, including the simplification of the higher-order mixed moments under $H_0$, the explicit 
covariance matrix entries, and the delta-method calculations, is provided in Appendix~\ref{app:nominal-serial}.\\
In an exactly analogous way as for ordinal data (see Section~\ref{subsec:serial-testing}) a test for serial dependence on a given lag $h$
based on $\hat{\kappa}_{\mathrm{nom}}(h)$ may be constructed. The test statistic is given by
\[T_n(h):= \frac{\sqrt{n}\hat{\kappa}_{\mathrm{nom}}(h)}{\sigma_{\kappa}}.\] 
The consistency of the test under $H_A$ follows from the consistency of $\hat{p}_i^{*}$ and $\hat{p}_{ii}^{(h)*}$
combined with the same continuous mapping argument as for ordinal data (see Section~\ref{subsec:serial-HA}).

\chapter*{Conclusion and Outlook}
\addcontentsline{toc}{chapter}{Conclusion and Outlook}

This thesis developed asymptotic theory for the estimation of 
distributional characteristics of categorical time series under 
partial observability, building on and extending the amplitude 
modulation framework of \citet{weiss2021categorical}. For ordinal 
processes, we established the asymptotic normality of the 
missingness-corrected estimator $\hat{\boldsymbol{f}}^*$ under 
general dependence conditions, derived the resulting 
asymptotic properties of the IOV and skewness estimators via the 
delta method, and obtained explicit formulas to approximate the finite-sample bias. 
For the analysis of serial dependence, we constructed a 
corrected estimator of the bivariate lag-$h$ probabilities, derived 
the closed-form asymptotic variance of $\hat{\kappa}_{\mathrm{ord}}(h)$ 
under the null hypothesis of serial independence, and showed that the 
resulting test remains consistent under the alternative. All results 
were extended to nominal processes, where the PMF and the IQV take 
the role of the CDF and the IOV, respectively. The accompanying Monte 
Carlo simulation study, based on the BinAR(1) process, confirmed the 
accuracy of the asymptotic approximations already at moderate sample 
sizes and illustrated the importance of the dependence assumptions by showing 
how the asymptotic approximations deteriorate when these assumptions 
are violated. \\
Several limitations of the present work point to natural directions 
for future research.

\paragraph{Asymptotic theory under $H_A$.} As discussed in 
\Cref{subsec:serial-testing}, the asymptotic variance of 
$\hat{\kappa}_{\mathrm{ord}}(h)$ is only available in closed form 
under the null hypothesis of serial independence, since the i.i.d.\ 
assumption on $(X_t)$ eliminates the higher-order mixed moments that 
otherwise appear in $\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{\boldsymbol{f}^{(h)*}}$. 
Consequently, no confidence interval for the true value 
$\kappa_{\mathrm{ord}}(h)$ can currently be constructed under the 
alternative. A natural extension would be to impose additional 
structure on $(X_t)$ --- specifically by assuming some general model 
under which the relevant higher-order moments become tractable.  Care 
must be taken, however, to strike a reasonable balance between 
tractability and generality: the chosen model should be specific 
enough to permit closed-form expressions for the relevant moments, 
yet general enough to remain practically relevant rather than being 
tailored to a narrow and largely artificial special case. A nonparametric
alternative would be to estimate the long-run variance directly via the 
higher-order moments entering $\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{\boldsymbol{f}^{(h)*}}$.
Since $m^2$ moment terms must be estimated for each lag and the corresponding 
long-run variance involves a theoretically infinite sum over lags, practical 
implementation would require lag truncation. Consequently, the approach may 
become demanding for large state spaces or limited sample sizes.

\paragraph{Missingness beyond MCAR.} The asymptotic theory developed 
in this thesis rests on the assumption that $(O_t)$ and $(X_t)$ are 
independent, corresponding to the MCAR case from \Cref{sec:missingness}. The simulation results 
in \Cref{sec:mnar} demonstrate that this assumption is not merely a 
technical convenience: if the independence assumption is violated, 
the corrected estimators $\hat{\boldsymbol{f}}^*$ and $\hat{\boldsymbol{f}}^{(h)*}$ 
are no longer consistent, and the resulting bias propagates to
plug-in estimators like the IOV and $\hat{\kappa}_{\mathrm{ord}}(h)$. 
Modelling missingness under MNAR is inherently difficult, since by 
definition the missingness mechanism depends on precisely the values 
that are unobserved. Under MAR, however, where the observation 
probability depends only on the observed values, or, more specifically for time series,
on the observed history of the process, a 
formal asymptotic theory appears feasible under suitable model 
assumptions. A natural next step could therefore be to develop such a 
theory for the MAR case, where the observation probability is allowed 
to depend on the history of the process but not on the missing values 
themselves.

\paragraph{Extensions to multivariate and mixed-type processes.} 
The present thesis focuses on a single categorical process $(X_t)$ 
observed under missingness. A complementary direction for future research 
would be to consider multivariate categorical time series, where several categorical 
variables are observed jointly at each time point and may be subject 
to missingness individually or jointly. This raises new questions 
regarding the appropriate notion of serial and cross-sectional 
dependence, as well as the interplay between the missingness 
mechanisms of the different component processes. A further direction 
would be to study mixed-type multivariate time series, in which some 
components are categorical and others are continuous or count-valued, 
again under partial observability. Such settings are common in 
applied contexts --- for instance large panel surveys that record categorical questions
alongside continuous measurements --- and would require 
combining the framework developed here with the existing asymptotic 
theory for missingness in metric time series.\\
Taken together, the results of this thesis provide a rigorous 
foundation for the analysis of categorical time series under 
partial observability and clarify both the strengths and the limits 
of the amplitude modulation framework. The simulation evidence on 
MNAR sensitivity, in particular, underscores that the plausibility of 
the MCAR assumption should be carefully assessed in any applied 
setting before the proposed estimators are used for inference.

\section{Asymptotic Variance and Bias of $\hat{\boldsymbol{p}}^*$}
\label{app:pstar}

The asymptotic properties of $\hat{\boldsymbol{p}}^*$ follow from the
delta method by exactly the same argument as in
Section~\ref{sec:fstar_hat}. Defining the vector-valued function
$\boldsymbol{g}\colon [0,1]^{m+2} \to [0,1]^{m+1}$ by
\[
    g_j(x_{-1}, x_0, \ldots, x_m) = x_j / x_{-1},
    \qquad j = 0, \ldots, m,
\]
and evaluating the Jacobian at
$\boldsymbol{\mu}_{\boldsymbol{Y}^*} := \pi(1, \boldsymbol{p}^\top)^\top$
yields
\[
    \boldsymbol{G} =
    \begin{pmatrix}
        -\frac{p_0}{\pi} & \frac{1}{\pi} & 0 & \ldots & 0\\
        -\frac{p_1}{\pi} & 0 & \frac{1}{\pi} & \ddots & \vdots \\
        \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \ddots & 0\\
        -\frac{p_{m}}{\pi} & 0 & \ldots & 0 & \frac{1}{\pi}
    \end{pmatrix},
\]
which has the same structure as the Jacobian in
Section~\ref{sec:fstar_hat}, with $f_i$ replaced by $p_i$ throughout.
Since the elements of $\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{\boldsymbol{Y}^*}$ are
obtained from those of $\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{\boldsymbol{Z}^*}$ by
replacing $f_i$, $f_{ij}(h)$, and $(f_{\min\{i,j\}} - f_if_j)$ with
$p_i$, $p_{ij}(h)$, and $p_j(\delta_{ij} - p_i)$ respectively, it
follows by the same matrix multiplication as in
Section~\ref{sec:fstar_hat} that the elements of
$\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{\hat{\boldsymbol{p}}^*}$ are obtained from those
of $\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{\hat{\boldsymbol{f}}^*}$ by the same
substitution.\\
For the bias, the Hessian of $g_j$ evaluated at
$\boldsymbol{\mu}_{\boldsymbol{Y}^*}$ is sparse, with non-zero entries
\[
    \boldsymbol{H}_{g_j} =
    \begin{cases}
        \dfrac{2p_j}{\pi^2}  & \text{if } i = k = -1, \\[1ex]
        -\dfrac{1}{\pi^2}    & \text{if } i = -1,\ k = j, \\[1ex]
        0                    & \text{else,}
    \end{cases}
\]
which is identical in structure to the ordinal case. Applying
Lemma~\ref{lem:quadform} and using
$\sigma_{\boldsymbol{Y}^*;\,-1,j} = p_j\,\sigma_{\boldsymbol{Y}^*;\,-1,-1}$
from the nominal analogue of case~\textbf{(ii)} of
Remark~\ref{rem:sigma_zstar}, the bias reduces to
\begin{align*}
    \mathrm{Bias}(\hat{p}_j)
    &= \frac{1}{2}\,\mathrm{tr}
       \bigl(\boldsymbol{H}_{g_j}\,\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{\boldsymbol{Y}^*}\bigr) \\
    &= \frac{p_j}{\pi^2}\,\sigma_{\boldsymbol{Y}^*;\,-1,-1}
       - \frac{1}{\pi^2}\,p_j\,\sigma_{\boldsymbol{Y}^*;\,-1,-1}
     = 0.
\end{align*}
Hence, $\hat{\boldsymbol{p}}^*$ is unbiased to order $n^{-1}$ under
MCAR, in direct analogy to the ordinal result of
Section~\ref{sec:fstar_hat}.
\section{Asymptotics of $\hat{\kappa}_{\mathrm{nom}}(h)$}\label{app:nominal-serial}

    &\textbf{(i)}\ i,j = 0, \ldots, m: \\
    &\quad \sigma^{(h)*}_{ij}
    = \frac{p_i p_j}{\pi^2} \sigma^{(h)}_{-1-1} 
    - \frac{p_j}{\pi^2} \sigma^{(h)}_{i-1} 
    - \frac{p_i}{\pi^2} \sigma^{(h)}_{-1j} 
    + \frac{1}{\pi^2} \sigma^{(h)}_{ij} \\
    &\quad = \frac{1}{\pi^2}(-p_i p_j \sigma^{(h)}_{-1-1} + p_i p_j \sigma^{(h)}_{-1-1} + \pi p_{j}(\delta_{ij} - p_i) + \sum_{k=1}^{\infty}\pi(k)(f_{ij}(k) + f_{ji}(k) - 2f_i f_j)) \\
    &\quad \overset{H_0}{=} \frac{1}{\pi} p_j(\delta_{ij} - p_i).\\
        %
    &\textbf{(ii)}\ i = 0, \ldots, m\ j = m+1, \ldots, 2m+2  (j' = j - m-1): \\
    &\quad \sigma^{(h)*}_{ij}
    = -\frac{p_{j'j'}(h)}{\pi(h)}\Bigl(\frac{- p_i}{\pi}\sigma^{(h)}_{-1-2} + \frac{1}{\pi}\sigma_{i-2}^{(h)}\Bigr)+ \frac{1}{\pi(h)}(-\frac{p_{i}}{\pi}\sigma_{-1j}^{(h)}+ \frac{1}{\pi}\sigma_{ij}^{(h)}) \\
    &\quad \overset{H_0}{=} \frac{1}{\pi(h)\pi}( p_i p_{j'}^2 \sigma_{-1-2}^{(h)} - p_{j'}^2 p_i\sigma_{-1-2}^{(h)} - p_i p_{j'}^2\sigma_{-1-2}^{(h)} + 2\pi(h)(\delta_{ij}p_i^2-p_i p_{j'}^2) + p_i p_{j'}^2\sigma_{-1-2}^{(h)}))   \\
    &\quad =\frac{2}{\pi}(\delta_{ij}p_i^2-p_i p_{j'}^2) = 2 p_{j'} \sigma^{(h)*}_{ij'} \\
    &\textbf{(iii)}\ i,j = m+1, \ldots, 2m+2 
    \ (i' = i-m-1,\ j' = j-m-1): \\
    &\quad \sigma^{(h)*}_{ij}
    = -\frac{p_{j'j'}(h)}{\pi(h)}\left(\frac{-p_{i'i'}(h)}{\pi(h)}\sigma_{-2-2}^{(h)}+ \frac{1}{\pi(h)}\sigma_{i-2}^{(h)}\right) + \frac{1}{\pi(h)}\left(\frac{-p_{i'i'}(h)}{\pi(h)}\sigma_{-2j}^{(h)}+ \frac{1}{\pi(h)}\sigma_{ij}^{(h)}\right)\\
    &\quad \overset{H_0}{=} \frac{1}{\pi(h)^2} \left( p_{i'}^2 p_{j'}^2 \sigma_{-2 -2}^{(h)} - p_{i'}^2 p_{j'}^2 \sigma_{-2 -2}^{(h)} - p_{i'}^2 p_{j'}^2 \sigma_{-2 -2}^{(h)}  + \sigma^{(h)}_{ij} \right) \\
    &\quad = \frac{1}{\pi(h)^2}  \left(\sigma_{ij}^{(h)} -p_{i'}^2 p_{j'}^2 \sigma_{-2-2}^{(h)}\right) \\
    &\quad = \frac{1}{\pi(h)^2}\bigl(\pi(h)p_{j'}^2(\delta_{i'j'} - p_{i'}^2) + 2\pi(h, 2h) (\delta_{i'j'} p_{j'}^3- p_{i'}^2p_{j'}^2)\bigr) \\
    &\quad = \frac{1}{\pi(h)^2}\bigl(\pi(h)p_{j'}^2\delta_{i'j'} - \pi(h)p_{i'}^2 p_{j'}^2 + 2\pi(h, 2h)\delta_{i'j'} p_{j'}^3 - 2\pi(h, 2h) p_{i'}^2 p_{j'}^2\bigr) \\
    &\quad = \frac{1}{\pi(h)^2}\bigl(\pi(h)p_{j'}^2(\delta_{i'j'} - p_{i'})(\delta_{i'j'} + p_{i'}) + 2\pi(h, 2h) p_{j'}^2p_{i'}(\delta_{i'j'}  - p_{i'})\bigr)\\
    &\quad = \frac{1}{\pi(h)^2}(\delta_{i'j'} - p_{i'})p_{j'}^2\bigl(\pi(h)(\delta_{i'j'} + p_{i'}) + 2\pi(h, 2h) p_{i'}\bigr) \\
\end{align*}
From here, we once again use the delta method to obtain closed-form expressions for the asymptotic variance and bias of $\hat{\kappa}_{\mathrm{nom}}(h) )= \frac{A}{B}$ under $H_0$,
where $A = \sum_{j = 0}^{m}(p_{jj}(h)-p_j^2)$ and $B= 1- \sum_{i = 0}^{m} p_i^2$. The corresponding 
Jacobian is given by
\[\boldsymbol{D}_\kappa:= (d_{10},\ldots, d_{1m}, d_{20},\ldots, d_{2m})\]
where for $i = 0, \ldots, m$:
\begin{align*}
    d_{1i} &= \frac{\partial \kappa_{\mathrm{nom}}}{\partial p_i} = \frac{-2p_i}{B} + \frac{2p_i A}{B^2},\\
    d_{2i} &= \frac{\partial \kappa_{\mathrm{nom}}}{\partial p_{ii}(h)} = \frac{1}{B}.
\end{align*}
Hence, the asymptotic variance of $\hat{\kappa}_{\mathrm{nom}}(h)$ under $H_0$ is given by


\begin{align*}
    \boldsymbol{D}_\kappa \boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{\boldsymbol{p}^{(h)*}} \boldsymbol{D}_\kappa^\top
    &= \sum_{i,j=0}^{m} d_{1i}\,d_{1j}\,\sigma^{(h)*}_{ij}
    + 2\sum_{i,j=0}^{m} d_{1i}\,d_{2j}\,\sigma^{(h)*}_{(i)\,(j+m+1)}
    + \sum_{i,j=0}^{m} d_{2i}\,d_{2j}\,\sigma^{(h)*}_{(i+m+1)(j+m+1)} \\
    &= \sum_{i,j=0}^{m} \left(\frac{-2 p_i}{B} + \frac{2 p_i A}{B^2}\right) \left(\frac{-2 p_j}{B} + \frac{2 p_j A}{B^2}\right)\,\sigma^{(h)*}_{ij} \\
    &\quad + 2\sum_{i,j=0}^{m} \left(\frac{-2 p_i}{B} + \frac{2 p_i A}{B^2}\right)\frac{1}{B}\,\sigma^{(h)*}_{i\,(j+m+1)} 
    + \sum_{i,j=0}^{m} \frac{1}{B^2}\,\sigma^{(h)*}_{(i+m+1)\,(j+m+1)} \\
    &\overset{H_0}{=} \frac{1}{B^2}\left(4\sum_{i,j=0}^{m} p_i p_j \,\sigma^{(h)*}_{ij}
    - 4\sum_{i,j=0}^{m} p_i \,\sigma^{(h)*}_{i\, (j+m+1)} 
    + \sum_{i,j=0}^{m} \,\sigma^{(h)*}_{(i+m+1)\,(j+m+1)}\right) \\
    &= \frac{1}{B^2}\biggl(
        4\sum_{i,j=0}^{m} p_i p_j \sigma^{(h)*}_{ij}\,
        - 8\sum_{i,j=0}^{m} p_i p_j \sigma^{(h)*}_{ij}
        + \frac{1}{\pi(h)^2} \sum_{i,j=0}^{m} (\delta_{ij}-p_i)p_j^2 \\    
        &\quad \cdot \bigl(\,\pi(h)(\delta_{ij} + p_i) +  2\pi(h, 2h)p_i\bigr)
      \biggr) \\
    &= \frac{1}{(1-\sum_{i=1}^{m}p_i^2)^2} \frac{1}{\pi(h)} \sum_{i, j = 0}^{m}p_j^2 
        (\delta_{ij}-p_i)\bigl(\delta_{ij} +  p_i (1+ 2\frac{\pi(h, 2h)}{\pi(h)} - 4\frac{\pi(h)}{\pi})\bigr).
\end{align*} 
\noindent For the bias we once again first need the Hessian of $\kappa_{\mathrm{nom}}(h)$ evaluated at $\boldsymbol{\mu}^{(h)*}$.
\begin{align*}
    h_{1i, 1j} &= \frac{\partial d_{1i}}{\partial p_j} = \frac{\partial}{\partial p_j} \bigl(\frac{-2p_i}{B} + \frac{2p_i A}{B^2}\bigr) 
    =\begin{cases} \frac{-2}{B} - \frac{8 p_i^2}{B^2} -2 \kappa_{ \mathrm{nom}}(h) (\frac{1}{B} - \frac{2p_i^2}{B^2})  & \text{if } i = j,\\
          -2(1-\kappa_{\mathrm{nom}}(h))\frac{4p_i p_j}{B^2} & \text{else.}
    \end{cases}, \\
    h_{1i, 2j} &= \frac{\partial d_{1i}}{\partial p_{jj}(h)} = \frac{2 p_i}{B^2},
    \quad h_{2i, 1j} = \frac{\partial d_{2i}}{\partial p_{j}} =  \frac{2p_j}{B^2},
    \quad h_{2i, 2j} = \frac{\partial d_{2i}}{\partial p_{jj}(h)} = 0.
\end{align*}
Where $h_{1i, 1j}$ is computed as follows 
\begin{align*}
\frac{\partial d_{1i}}{\partial p_j}
&=
\frac{\partial}{\partial p_j}
\left(
\frac{-2p_i}{B}
+
\frac{2p_iA}{B^2}
\right).
\end{align*}
\[
\begin{aligned}
i=j:\qquad
\frac{\partial d_{1i}}{\partial p_i}
&=
\frac{-2}{B}
-\frac{4p_i^2+2A+4p_i^2}{B^2}
-\frac{2p_iA\,2B(-2p_i)}{B^4} \\
&=
\frac{-2}{B}
-\frac{8p_i^2}{B^2}
-2\kappa_{\mathrm{nom}}(h)
\left(
\frac{1}{B}
-\frac{2p_i^2}{B^2}
\right),
\\[1em]
i\neq j:\qquad
\frac{\partial d_{1i}}{\partial p_j}
&=
\frac{-4p_ip_j}{B^2}
+
2p_i
\left(
\frac{-2p_j}{B^2}
-\frac{A\,2B(-2p_j)}{B^4}
\right) \\
&=
-2\bigl(1-\kappa_{\mathrm{nom}}(h)\bigr)
\frac{4p_ip_j}{B^2}.
\end{aligned}
\]
Under $H_0$ we have $\kappa_{\mathrm{nom}}(h) = 0$ and therefore $h_{1i, 1j}$ simplifies:
\[h_{1i, 1j}\overset{H_0}{=}\begin{cases} \frac{-2 }{B} - \frac{8p_i^2}{B^2}& \text{if } i = j,\\
          \frac{-8 p_i p_j }{B^2} & \text{else.}
    \end{cases}\]
A bias correction of order $n^{-1}$ can be derived using a second-order Taylor expansion of $\kappa_{\mathrm{nom}}$ around $\boldsymbol{\mu}^{(h)*}$. 
Note that since $\kappa_{\mathrm{nom}}$ is not a quadratic function of $\boldsymbol{\mu}^{(h)∗}$, higher-order 
derivatives do not vanish. Consequently, further terms of the Taylor expansion would yield 
bias corrections beyond order $n^{-1}$. The bias is given by 
\[\mathrm{Bias}(\hat{\kappa}_{\mathrm{nom}}(h))=\frac{1}{n2}\mathrm{tr}\bigl[\boldsymbol{H}_\kappa\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{\boldsymbol{p}^{(h)*}}\bigr] + o(n^{-1}),\]
where the remainder term $o(n^{-1})$ collects all contributions arising from higher-order derivatives of $\hat{\kappa}_{\mathrm{nom}}$, 
which are of smaller asymptotic order than $n^{-1}$. The present derivation is restricted to the leading bias term, which is sufficient for the asymptotic considerations of this thesis.
Using Lemma~\ref{lem:quadform} and the fact that $\sigma^{(h)*}_{i\,(j+m+1)} = 2 p_j \sigma^{(h)*}_{ij}$ from case \textbf{(ii)}, the leading bias term reduces to
\begin{align*}
    \frac{1}{n2}\mathrm{tr}\bigl[\boldsymbol{H}_\kappa\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{\boldsymbol{p}^{(h)*}}\bigr] 
    &=\frac{1}{n2}\sum_{i=0}^{2m+2}\bigl[\boldsymbol{H}_\kappa\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{\boldsymbol{p}^{(h)*}}\bigr]_{ii}  \\
    &=\frac{1}{n2}\Biggl[\sum_{i,  j =0}^{m}h_{1i,1j}\sigma^{(h)*}_{ij}+h_{1i,2j}\sigma^{(h)*}_{i(j+m+1)}
    +h_{2i,1j}\sigma^{(h)*}_{(i+m+1)j}\Biggr]\\
    &= \frac{1}{n2}\Biggl[\sum_{i = 0}^{m}(\frac{-2}{B} - \frac{8p_i^2}{B^2}) \frac{1}{\pi}p_i(1-p_i)
     + \sum_{\substack{i,j = 0\\ i \neq j}}^{m} \frac{-8 p_i p_j }{B^2} \frac{1}{\pi}p_j(0-p_i) \\
    &\quad + 2 \sum_{i, j = 0}^{m} \frac{2p_i}{B^2} 2p_j \frac{1}{\pi} (\delta_{ij} p_i -  p_i p_j) \Biggr]  \\
    &= \frac{1}{ n \pi}\biggl[\sum_{i = 0}^{m} \biggl(\frac{- p_i + p_i^2 }{B} - \frac{4 p_i^3 + 4 p_i^4 }{B^2} + \frac{4p_i^3- 4p_i^4}{B^2}\biggr) \biggr] \\
    &= \frac{1}{n\pi} \frac{-B}{B} = - \frac{1}{n \pi} 
\end{align*}
where we used that $h_{2i, 2j} = 0$ in the third equation. We can conclude that the finite-sample bias is given by
\[\mathrm{Bias}(\hat{\kappa}_{\mathrm{nom}}(h)) = -\frac{1}{n \pi} + o(n^{-1}).\]
